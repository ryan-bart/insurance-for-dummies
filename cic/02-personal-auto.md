## Why This Matters

Auto insurance is the most litigated, most regulated, and most price-sensitive personal line. Getting it wrong creates E&O exposure for agents. The CIC approach: know the form cold, understand the scenarios agents face daily, and spot the traps before they become claims.

---

## "Who Is an Insured" — The Scenarios Agents Face

This is the #1 source of confusion and E&O claims in personal auto. The PAP defines insureds in layers:

### The Hierarchy

| Level | Who | For Which Vehicles |
|-------|-----|-------------------|
| **1** | Named insured + spouse | **Any** auto (owned or non-owned) |
| **2** | Family members (residents related by blood/marriage/adoption) | **Any** auto |
| **3** | Anyone using the covered auto | Only the **covered auto**, with reasonable belief of permission |
| **4** | Anyone for liability arising from Level 1 or 2 | Only the **covered auto** |

### Real Scenarios

**Scenario**: Your client's 19-year-old borrows a roommate's car and causes an accident.
- **Is the 19-year-old a family member?** If yes (lives at home, related) → covered under the parent's PAP for any auto.
- **If not a family member** (moved out, independent) → NOT covered under the parent's PAP. Must rely on the roommate's policy or their own.

**Scenario**: A neighbor borrows your client's car to run errands and causes an accident.
- The neighbor is an insured under **your client's PAP** (Level 3 — using covered auto with permission).
- Your client's policy is **primary**. The neighbor's own policy is **excess**.
- **Insurance follows the car first, then the driver.**

**Scenario**: Your client's car is in the shop. They're driving a loaner from the dealer.
- **Temporary substitute vehicle** — treated as a covered auto.
- Full coverage applies as if it were the insured's own car.

## Covered Auto Definition

What counts as a "covered auto":

1. **Vehicles shown on the declarations page**
2. **Newly acquired vehicles** — automatic coverage, but:
   - If you already have a vehicle of that type: coverage extends automatically for the policy period (but notify the insurer)
   - If it's an additional vehicle (broadening coverage): **14 days** to notify (30 days in some policies)
3. **Temporary substitute vehicles** — loaners, rentals while your car is being repaired
4. **Trailers** — coverage for owned trailers

**Agent trap**: Client buys a new car Friday afternoon. Wraps it around a pole Saturday. If they didn't notify you and it's an additional vehicle (not a replacement), coverage may only last 14 days — and that clock started at purchase, not at the accident.

## Coverage Parts: Real Client Scenarios

### Part A — Liability

**Scenario**: Your client runs a red light and T-bones another car. Three occupants are injured. Total claim: $450,000.

With 100/300/50 limits:
- Per person BI: $100K max each
- Per accident BI: $300K max total
- PD: $50K max

If the three claimants get $200K, $150K, and $100K awards:
- Person 1: $100K (capped) — your client owes $100K personally
- Person 2: $100K (capped) — your client owes $50K personally
- Person 3: $100K (capped at per-accident remaining $100K)
- Your client is personally exposed for **$150K out of pocket**

**Agent lesson**: Always recommend limits high enough to support an umbrella. 100/300/50 is dangerously low for most clients.

### Part B — Medical Payments

- Pays regardless of fault
- Your client + passengers in the covered auto
- Your client as a pedestrian struck by a vehicle
- Typical limits: $1,000–$10,000
- Treatment within 3 years of accident

**Agent tip**: In no-fault states, PIP replaces Med Pay. PIP is broader (lost wages, essential services, funeral). Know which one your state uses.

### Part C — Uninsured/Underinsured Motorists

**UM scenario**: Hit-and-run on the highway. No other driver identified. Your client has $50K in medical bills.
- UM responds. The hit-and-run qualifies as an "uninsured" motorist.
- Client must report promptly and cooperate.

**UIM scenario**: Other driver has $25K limits. Your client's injuries are $150K. Your client's UIM limit is $100K.
- Other driver's policy pays $25K
- UIM pays up to $100K (or $100K minus the $25K = $75K, depending on offset vs non-offset state)

**Stacking**: If your client has three cars on the policy, can they stack UM/UIM limits?
- **Stacking state**: Yes. Three cars × $100K = $300K available
- **Non-stacking state**: No. $100K regardless of how many vehicles
- **Know your state's rule** — this is a major E&O exposure

### Part D — Physical Damage

Two coverages:
- **Collision**: Your car hits something (or something hits your car, or it overturns)
- **Other Than Collision (OTC / Comprehensive)**: Everything else — theft, fire, hail, flood, vandalism, animal strike, glass, falling objects

**The animal rule**: Hitting a deer = OTC (not collision). This matters because:
1. OTC claims often don't raise rates
2. OTC deductibles may be different from collision deductibles

**Total loss**: When repair cost exceeds the threshold (usually 70-80% of ACV), the vehicle is totaled. Insurer pays ACV minus deductible.

**Gap coverage**: If the client owes $28K on a loan but the car's ACV is $22K, they're $6K short after a total loss. Gap insurance covers the difference.

## PAP Exclusions That Trip Up Agents

| Exclusion | The Trap |
|-----------|---------|
| **Vehicles with fewer than 4 wheels** | Client buys a motorcycle, assumes it's covered. It's not — needs a separate motorcycle policy |
| **Vehicles used as public conveyance** | Client starts driving for Uber/Lyft. PAP excludes it. Need rideshare endorsement or commercial coverage |
| **Business-owned vehicles** | Client's LLC owns the car. PAP is personal — need a commercial auto policy |
| **Vehicles used in auto business** | Client works at a dealership and drives customer cars. Not covered for non-owned vehicles used in auto business |
| **Racing** | Client takes their car to a track day. Excluded |
| **Rental/leased to others** | Client rents their car on Turo. Excluded — need commercial or platform coverage |

## How the PAP Interacts with Other Policies

| Situation | Which Policy Is Primary |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Client driving their own car | Their PAP |
| Client driving someone else's car | Owner's PAP is primary; client's PAP is excess |
| Someone driving client's car (with permission) | Client's PAP is primary |
| Client driving a rental car | Client's PAP is primary (if they have comp/collision); rental company's coverage is secondary |
| Employer-provided vehicle | Employer's commercial auto is primary |

## State-Specific Issues Agents Must Know

### Minimum Limits Vary Wildly

| State | Minimum BI/PD |
|-------|--------------|
| California | 15/30/5 |
| Florida | 10/20/10 (PIP required, BI not required) |
| New York | 25/50/10 |
| Texas | 30/60/25 |
| Alaska | 50/100/25 |

**Agent lesson**: Minimum limits are never enough. They exist for financial responsibility compliance, not actual protection.

### No-Fault vs. Tort States
- **No-fault (12 states + Puerto Rico)**: Your own PIP pays your injuries first. Can only sue if injuries exceed a threshold.
- **Tort (majority)**: The at-fault driver's liability coverage pays the injured party's damages.
- **Choice states (KY, NJ, PA)**: Insureds can choose between no-fault and tort.

## Common E&O Traps for Auto Agents

1. **Not asking about all household drivers** — undisclosed drivers can void coverage
2. **Not updating for newly acquired vehicles** — client assumes they're covered
3. **Not recommending UM/UIM** — in states where it's optional, clients reject it to save money, then get hit by an uninsured driver
4. **Not recommending adequate limits** — minimum limits create massive personal exposure
5. **Not asking about rideshare/delivery driving** — standard PAP excludes it
6. **Not documenting coverage recommendations** — if you recommended higher limits and the client declined, document it

## Practice Questions

**1.** Your client lends her car to a coworker. The coworker causes a $200K accident. Whose insurance pays first?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Your client's PAP — insurance follows the car first. The coworker is an insured under your client's policy (permissive user of the covered auto). The coworker's own PAP would be excess if your client's limits aren't sufficient.
</details>

**2.** A client in a stacking state has two cars on their PAP, each with $100K UM. They're hit by an uninsured driver and have $180K in injuries. How much UM coverage is available?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
$200,000 (2 cars × $100K). In a stacking state, the UM limits are added together across all vehicles on the policy. The $180K claim is fully covered.
</details>

**3.** Your client starts driving for DoorDash using their personal vehicle. Are they covered under their standard PAP?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
No — using a vehicle for delivery services (public conveyance / business use) is excluded. They need a rideshare/delivery endorsement or a commercial auto policy. This is a major gap that agents need to ask about proactively.
</details>

**4.** A client hits a deer, causing $6,000 in damage. They have a $500 collision deductible and a $250 comprehensive deductible. What do they pay?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
$250. Hitting an animal is classified as Other Than Collision (comprehensive), not collision. The OTC deductible of $250 applies, not the collision deductible.
</details>

**5.** A client buys a second car on Saturday and doesn't tell their agent. On Monday, the new car is stolen. Is it covered?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
It depends on the policy terms for newly acquired vehicles. If they already had physical damage coverage on their existing car, OTC coverage (which covers theft) typically extends automatically to additional vehicles for 14 days (some policies allow 30). They'd be covered — but they still need to notify the insurer promptly. If they had no physical damage coverage on existing vehicles, no coverage would extend.
</details>
