# CPCU 556: Personal Risk Assessment

## Why This Matters

CPCU 556 shifts perspective — from understanding coverages to understanding how insurers think. Risk assessment is the foundation: before you can price, underwrite, or sell a policy, you have to identify and evaluate what can go wrong.

---

## The Four Categories of Personal Loss Exposures

### 1. Property Loss Exposures
What you own that can be damaged, destroyed, or stolen.

- **Real property**: Home, other structures, land improvements
- **Personal property**: Contents, vehicles, valuables
- **Key considerations**: Replacement cost, location, construction type, susceptibility to catastrophe

### 2. Liability Loss Exposures
Situations where you could be legally responsible for harming others.

- **Premises liability**: Someone gets hurt on your property
- **Auto liability**: You cause an accident
- **Personal activities**: Sports, social media, pet ownership
- **Vicarious liability**: Liability for the acts of others (your kids, your employees)
- **Key consideration**: Liability losses are potentially unlimited — no cap on what a jury can award

### 3. Income/Earnings Loss Exposures
Threats to your ability to earn money.

- **Disability**: Illness or injury preventing work
- **Death**: Loss of a breadwinner's income
- **Job loss**: Not insurable in the traditional sense but part of risk planning
- **Retirement**: Outliving your savings (longevity risk)

### 4. Health/Medical Loss Exposures
Costs of medical care and long-term care.

- **Acute illness or injury**: Hospital, surgery, emergency
- **Chronic conditions**: Ongoing treatment and medication
- **Long-term care**: Custodial care needs in old age
- **Key consideration**: Medical costs are a leading contributor to personal bankruptcy in the U.S. — research estimates roughly two-thirds of bankruptcy filers cite medical issues as a key factor

## The Risk Management Process for Individuals

The same framework insurers use applies to personal risk management:

### Step 1: Identify Loss Exposures
Catalog everything that could go wrong. Think through:
- What do you own? (property)
- What do you do? (activities creating liability)
- Who depends on your income? (life/disability needs)
- What health risks do you face? (medical/LTC)

### Step 2: Analyze Loss Exposures
For each exposure, assess:
- **Frequency**: How likely is this to happen?
- **Severity**: How bad would it be financially?

| | Low Severity | High Severity |
|---|---|---|
| **High Frequency** | Retain (budget for it) | Avoid or reduce |
| **Low Frequency** | Retain | **Transfer (insure)** |

The sweet spot for insurance is **low frequency, high severity** — unlikely events that would be financially devastating.

### Step 3: Select Risk Management Techniques

| Technique | What It Means | Example |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| **Avoidance** | Don't engage in the activity | Don't buy a trampoline |
| **Loss prevention** | Reduce frequency | Install smoke detectors |
| **Loss reduction** | Reduce severity | Keep a fire extinguisher |
| **Retention** | Accept the risk and pay out of pocket | Choose a higher deductible |
| **Transfer** | Shift risk to someone else | Buy insurance |
| **Non-insurance transfer** | Shift risk contractually | Hold-harmless agreement, warranty |

### Step 4: Implement
Put the plan into action — buy the policies, install the deadbolts, set up the emergency fund.

### Step 5: Monitor and Revise
Life changes. Review annually or when:
- You buy/sell property
- Family size changes
- Income changes significantly
- You start a business or side hustle
- You acquire new assets (boat, rental property)

## Determining Appropriate Coverage Levels

### Property Coverage
- **Dwelling**: 100% of replacement cost (not market value)
- **Contents**: Default percentages may be insufficient — do a home inventory
- **Deductibles**: Higher deductible = lower premium, but make sure you can cover it

### Liability Coverage
- **Minimum**: At least enough to cover your net worth
- **Better**: Net worth + future earnings potential
- **Best**: Maximum available through an umbrella — it's cheap relative to the protection

### Life Insurance
- **Common rules of thumb**: 10x income, or enough to replace income for X years
- **Better approach**: Calculate actual needs — mortgage payoff, education costs, income replacement, final expenses, minus existing assets

### Disability Insurance
- Target **60–70% of gross income**
- Own-occupation definition if available
- Benefit period to age 65

## Common Coverage Gaps

| Gap | Why It Happens | Solution |
|-----|---------------|----------|
| Underinsured dwelling | Haven't updated since purchase/renovation | Annual replacement cost review |
| No flood coverage | "I'm not in a flood zone" | NFIP or private flood (over 25% of NFIP flood claims come from outside high-risk zones) |
| Jewelry/valuables over sub-limits | Don't realize sub-limits exist | Scheduled personal property |
| Inadequate liability limits | $100K seems like a lot until it's not | Umbrella policy |
| No disability insurance | "It won't happen to me" | Individual DI policy |
| No umbrella | Don't know they exist or think they're expensive | Education + quote |
| Gaps in auto coverage | Minimum state limits only | Increase to match umbrella requirements |

## Practice Questions

**1.** A married couple with two kids, a $400K mortgage, and $150K combined income wants to know how much life insurance they need. What's a reasonable starting point?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
At minimum, enough to cover the mortgage ($400K) plus several years of income replacement. A common approach: $400K mortgage + 10 years of income ($1.5M) = ~$1.9M total. Subtract existing savings and employer-provided life insurance. Each spouse should be evaluated separately based on their income contribution.
</details>

**2.** Your client says "I don't need flood insurance — I'm in Zone X." What do you tell them?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
FEMA data shows that over 25% of NFIP flood claims come from outside high-risk zones (Zones A and V) — recent 10-year data puts it closer to one-third. Zone X means lower risk, not no risk. NFIP premiums in Zone X are also much lower, making it an affordable precaution. Also, it only takes 1 inch of water to cause $25,000+ in damage.
</details>

**3.** Which risk management technique is insurance?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Transfer. Insurance transfers the financial consequences of a loss from the individual to the insurer in exchange for a premium. It's most appropriate for low-frequency, high-severity exposures.
</details>

**4.** A family has a $500,000 home, $200,000 in savings, $100K/year income, and a $100,000 HO liability limit. What's the coverage gap?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The liability limit is far too low. Their net worth alone is $700K+, plus future earnings. A single serious liability claim could exceed $100K easily. They need to increase HO liability to at least $300K-$500K and add a $1M+ umbrella policy.
</details>

**5.** When should a personal risk management plan be reviewed?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Annually at minimum, plus any time there's a significant life change: marriage/divorce, birth of a child, home purchase/sale, major income change, new business/side hustle, acquiring high-value assets, or reaching retirement age.
</details>
