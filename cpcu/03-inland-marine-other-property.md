# CPCU 555: Inland Marine & Other Personal Property Coverage

---

## Why This Matters

Homeowners policies have sub-limits on valuable items and don't cover everything. Inland marine and specialty property coverages fill the gaps — especially for high-value items, mobile property, and unique exposures.

---

## What Is "Inland Marine"?

The name is historical: marine insurance covered goods in transit over water. "Inland marine" extended that to goods in transit or mobile property over land. Today it covers:

- Property that moves around
- Property of unusual value
- Property that doesn't fit neatly into standard homeowners or auto policies

---

## Personal Inland Marine Coverages

### Scheduled Personal Property (Personal Articles Floater)

The most common personal inland marine coverage. It "schedules" (individually lists and values) specific high-value items.

**How it works:**
- Each item is listed with an agreed value
- Coverage is typically **open peril** (broader than HO)
- **No deductible** in most cases
- Covers items anywhere in the world

**Common categories:** Jewelry, fine arts, musical instruments, cameras, silverware, furs, stamp/coin collections, golf equipment

**Why you need it:** The HO policy limits jewelry to $1,500. A $15,000 engagement ring needs a floater.

### Personal Property Floater (Unscheduled)

Provides a blanket amount of open-peril coverage on all personal property without itemizing.

- Usually has a deductible
- May not cover as high a value per item
- Easier to administer but less precise

### Personal Effects Floater

Covers unscheduled personal property of travelers. Less common today but historically covered luggage and belongings during travel.

## Watercraft and Boat Insurance

### What HO Covers
- Watercraft liability for boats under 26 feet with limited horsepower
- $1,500 personal property sub-limit for watercraft

### What Requires Separate Coverage
- Boats over 26 feet or high-horsepower
- Sailboats over 26 feet
- Personal watercraft (jet skis)
- Any boat used commercially

### Boat Owner's Policy
- **Hull coverage**: Physical damage to the vessel
- **Liability**: BI/PD caused by the boat
- **Medical payments**: For passengers
- **Uninsured boater**: Like UM for cars
- **Trailer coverage**
- Navigation warranties may apply (geographic and seasonal limits)

---

## Recreational Vehicle Coverage

Vehicles not covered under a standard PAP: ATVs, snowmobiles, golf carts, dirt bikes, motorcycles. These need their own policies with liability, physical damage, medical payments, and UM/UIM.

---

## Valuation Methods

| Method | How It Works | Best For |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| **Agreed Value** | Pre-agreed payout at total loss | Fine art, antiques, collectibles |
| **Actual Cash Value (ACV)** | Replacement cost minus depreciation | Standard personal property |
| **Replacement Cost** | Cost to replace with like kind/quality, no depreciation | Newer items, standard contents |
| **Functional Replacement Cost** | Cost to replace the function, not the exact item | Older/unique items |

Scheduled items need professional appraisals, updated every 2–3 years.

## Flood Insurance (NFIP)

Flood is excluded from homeowners. The National Flood Insurance Program is the primary source.

### Key Features
- **Building max**: $250,000 residential
- **Contents max**: $100,000
- **30-day waiting period** (exceptions for new purchase)
- Administered by FEMA, sold through private insurers ("Write Your Own")

### What It Covers
- Direct physical loss by flood (overflow of waters, mudflow, erosion from waves)
- Building: structure, HVAC, electrical, plumbing, appliances
- Contents: personal belongings (severely limited in basements)

### What It Doesn't Cover
- Moisture/mildew/mold that could have been avoided
- Currency, precious metals, stock certificates
- Property outside the building (landscaping, fences, pools)
- Vehicles or living expenses (no ALE)

### Private Flood Market
Growing alternative — often offers higher limits, replacement cost on contents, loss of use coverage, and fewer restrictions. May be more expensive in high-risk zones.

## Earthquake Coverage

Available as an endorsement or standalone policy.

### Key Features
- **Percentage deductible**: 5–25% of Coverage A (not flat dollar)
  - $400K home with 10% deductible = **$40,000 deductible**
- Covers dwelling, other structures, personal property
- May cover loss of use

### Who Needs It
- West Coast (California CEA program)
- New Madrid fault zone (central U.S.)
- Pacific Northwest
- Anyone in a seismically active area

## Practice Questions

**1.** Your HO policy has a $1,500 jewelry sub-limit. You own a $10,000 watch. How do you get full coverage?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Purchase a Scheduled Personal Property endorsement listing the watch at its appraised value. This provides open-peril coverage, typically with no deductible.
</details>

**2.** You own a 30-foot powerboat. Does your homeowners policy provide liability coverage?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
No. HO limits watercraft liability to boats under 26 feet with limited horsepower. A separate boat owner's policy is needed.
</details>

**3.** Your basement floods. NFIP pays for your furniture stored there — true or false?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
False. NFIP severely limits contents coverage in basements. Most personal property (furniture, electronics) is not covered there.
</details>

**4.** Earthquake coverage with a 15% deductible on a $500,000 home. Earthquake causes $100,000 in damage. What do you pay?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
$75,000. The deductible is 15% of Coverage A ($500K) = $75K. Insurer pays $25,000. The percentage deductible is based on Coverage A, not the loss amount.
</details>

**5.** What's the key advantage of agreed value over ACV for a fine art collection?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
No depreciation debate at claim time. The value was agreed upfront, so a total loss pays that amount without dispute over what the piece was "worth."
</details>
