## Why This Matters

Personal lines professionals need to understand life insurance even if they don't sell it. It's part of the complete personal risk picture — and the CPCU exam tests it. Life insurance protects against the financial consequences of premature death.

---

## The Two Big Categories

### Term Life Insurance

Pure death protection. No cash value. You pay premiums for a set period — if you die during that period, beneficiaries get the death benefit. If you don't, the policy expires.

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Duration** | 10, 15, 20, or 30 years (most common) |
| **Premiums** | Level during the term, then spike at renewal |
| **Cash value** | None |
| **Best for** | Temporary needs: mortgage protection, income replacement while kids are young |
| **Cost** | Cheapest form of life insurance |

**Types of term:**
- **Level term**: Death benefit and premium stay the same
- **Decreasing term**: Death benefit decreases over time (matches a declining mortgage)
- **Annual renewable term (ART)**: Renews each year at increasing premiums
- **Convertible term**: Can be converted to permanent insurance without new underwriting

### Permanent Life Insurance

Lasts your entire life (as long as premiums are paid). Builds **cash value** — a savings component that grows tax-deferred.

#### Whole Life
- **Fixed premiums** for life
- **Guaranteed death benefit**
- **Guaranteed cash value** growth
- May pay **dividends** (participating policies)
- Most conservative and predictable

#### Universal Life (UL)
- **Flexible premiums** — pay more or less within limits
- **Flexible death benefit** — can adjust up or down
- Cash value earns interest based on a **declared rate** (with a minimum guarantee)
- More flexibility than whole life, but more risk

#### Variable Life
- Cash value invested in **sub-accounts** (like mutual funds)
- **Investment risk is on the policyholder**
- Potential for higher returns — or losses
- Death benefit may fluctuate
- Requires a securities license to sell

#### Variable Universal Life (VUL)
- Combines flexibility of UL with investment options of variable
- Maximum flexibility and maximum risk
- Both premiums and investments are variable

## Key Policy Components

### Death Benefit
The amount paid to beneficiaries at death. Can be:
- **Level**: Stays the same
- **Increasing**: Grows over time (some UL policies)
- **Decreasing**: Shrinks over time (decreasing term)

### Cash Value
The savings component in permanent policies. Key features:
- Grows **tax-deferred**
- Can be borrowed against (policy loans)
- Can be surrendered for cash (minus surrender charges)
- Is NOT the same as the death benefit

### Premium Structure
- **Net premium**: Covers mortality cost and expenses
- **Gross premium**: Net premium plus insurer's loading (profit, admin, contingencies)
- Permanent insurance premiums are higher because part goes to cash value

## Beneficiary Designations

| Type | What It Means |
|------|--------------|
| **Primary** | First in line to receive the death benefit |
| **Contingent** | Receives if the primary beneficiary is deceased |
| **Revocable** | Can be changed by the policyholder at any time (most common) |
| **Irrevocable** | Cannot be changed without the beneficiary's consent |
| **Per stirpes** | If a beneficiary dies, their share goes to their children |
| **Per capita** | If a beneficiary dies, their share is split among surviving beneficiaries |

**Key exam point**: Life insurance proceeds generally bypass probate and go directly to named beneficiaries.

## Important Policy Provisions and Riders

### Standard Provisions
- **Grace period**: Usually 31 days to pay a late premium before the policy lapses
- **Incontestability clause**: After 2 years, the insurer can't void the policy for misstatements on the application (except fraud in some states)
- **Suicide clause**: No death benefit for suicide within first 2 years; premiums are returned instead
- **Misstatement of age/sex**: Benefit adjusted to what the premium would have purchased at the correct age/sex

### Common Riders
| Rider | What It Does |
|-------|-------------|
| **Waiver of Premium** | Waives premiums if you become disabled |
| **Accidental Death Benefit (ADB)** | Pays double (or more) if death is accidental |
| **Guaranteed Insurability** | Right to buy additional coverage at specified dates without proving insurability |
| **Accelerated Death Benefit** | Allows access to part of the death benefit if terminally ill |
| **Child Term Rider** | Provides term coverage on children |
| **Cost of Living** | Death benefit increases with inflation |

---

## Non-Forfeiture Options

If you stop paying premiums on a permanent policy, you don't lose everything. Non-forfeiture options let you use your accumulated cash value:

| Option | What Happens |
|--------|-------------|
| **Cash surrender** | You get the cash value (minus surrender charges) and the policy ends |
| **Reduced paid-up** | Buys a smaller permanent policy with no further premiums due |
| **Extended term** | Uses the cash value to buy term insurance at the original death benefit for as long as it lasts |

---

## Settlement Options

How the death benefit is paid out:

| Option | How It Works |
|--------|-------------|
| **Lump sum** | One payment (most common) |
| **Interest only** | Insurer holds the proceeds and pays interest; principal paid later |
| **Fixed period** | Equal payments over a set number of years |
| **Fixed amount** | Equal payments of a set dollar amount until money runs out |
| **Life income** | Payments for the beneficiary's lifetime (annuitization) |

---

## Tax Treatment Basics

- **Death benefits**: Generally **income-tax-free** to beneficiaries (IRC Section 101)
- **Cash value growth**: **Tax-deferred** (no tax until you withdraw)
- **Policy loans**: Generally **not taxable** (as long as the policy stays in force)
- **Surrenders**: Taxed on the gain (cash value minus total premiums paid)
- **Premiums**: **Not tax-deductible** for personal life insurance
- **Modified Endowment Contract (MEC)**: If you overfund a policy, it loses favorable tax treatment on withdrawals

## Practice Questions

**1.** A 35-year-old with two young kids and a mortgage wants affordable coverage for the next 20 years. What type of life insurance is most appropriate?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
20-year level term. It matches the coverage need (kids will be grown, mortgage paid down), and it's the most affordable option for a specific time period.
</details>

**2.** What happens to a whole life policy's cash value if the insured stops paying premiums after 15 years?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The insured can choose a non-forfeiture option: cash surrender (take the cash value), reduced paid-up (smaller policy, no more premiums), or extended term (same death benefit as term insurance for a limited period). The cash value isn't lost.
</details>

**3.** A policyholder dies by suicide 18 months after purchasing a life insurance policy. Does the insurer pay the death benefit?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
No. The suicide clause excludes coverage for suicide within the first 2 years. The insurer returns the premiums paid, but does not pay the death benefit.
</details>

**4.** Are life insurance death benefits taxable income to the beneficiary?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Generally no. Life insurance death benefits are received income-tax-free under IRC Section 101. However, if the policy was transferred for value (sold to someone), the exclusion may be lost.
</details>

**5.** What's the difference between a variable life policy and a universal life policy?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Universal life has flexible premiums and a cash value that earns a declared interest rate (with a minimum guarantee). Variable life invests the cash value in sub-accounts (like mutual funds) — the policyholder bears the investment risk but has potential for higher returns. Variable life requires a securities license to sell.
</details>
