# CPCU 556: Underwriting Personal Lines

## Why This Matters

Underwriting is the process of deciding who to insure, on what terms, and at what price. It's the engine of the insurance business — get it right and you're profitable. Get it wrong and you're paying claims you never should have accepted.

---

## The Underwriting Process

### Step 1: Gather Information

| Source | What It Reveals | Used For |
|--------|----------------|----------|
| **Application** | Basic info: name, address, property details, vehicles, drivers | Starting point for all underwriting |
| **Motor Vehicle Report (MVR)** | Driving history: violations, accidents, suspensions | Auto underwriting |
| **CLUE Report** | Comprehensive Loss Underwriting Exchange — 7 years of claims history | Both HO and auto |
| **Credit-Based Insurance Score** | Financial responsibility indicator | Both HO and auto (controversial but widely used) |
| **Inspections** | Physical condition of property | Homeowners |
| **Public records** | Court records, liens, bankruptcies | Risk character assessment |
| **Prior insurance history** | Gaps in coverage, cancellations | Both |

### Step 2: Evaluate the Risk

The underwriter assesses:
- **Hazards**: Conditions that increase the chance or severity of loss
  - **Physical hazard**: Tangible conditions (old wiring, swimming pool)
  - **Moral hazard**: Dishonest intent (arson for profit)
  - **Morale hazard**: Carelessness due to having insurance ("I have coverage, who cares")
  - **Legal hazard**: Court decisions or regulations that increase losses

### Step 3: Make a Decision

| Decision | What It Means |
|----------|--------------|
| **Accept (standard)** | Normal risk, standard pricing |
| **Accept (substandard)** | Higher risk, higher premium or coverage restrictions |
| **Accept with modifications** | Exclude certain coverages or require conditions (e.g., install deadbolts) |
| **Decline** | Risk doesn't meet guidelines |
| **Refer** | Send to a senior underwriter for review |

## Homeowners Underwriting Factors

### Property Factors

| Factor | Why It Matters |
|--------|---------------|
| **Construction type** | Frame burns easier than masonry. Log homes, unique construction = harder to rate |
| **Roof age and material** | Old roofs = higher wind/water damage risk. Insurers increasingly surcharge or decline old roofs |
| **Year built** | Older homes may have outdated wiring, plumbing, or not meet current code |
| **Location** | Proximity to coast, wildfire zones, flood zones, crime rates |
| **Protection class** | ISO rates fire departments 1–10 (1 = best). Distance to fire station and hydrant matters |
| **Square footage and replacement cost** | Determines the exposure amount |
| **Heating type** | Wood stoves, space heaters = higher fire risk |
| **Swimming pool** | Major liability exposure (especially unfenced) |
| **Dogs** | Certain breeds trigger surcharges or exclusions (pit bulls, Rottweilers — varies by insurer) |
| **Trampoline** | Significant liability concern |
| **Home-based business** | Changes the risk profile |

### Claims History (CLUE)
- **Frequency matters more than severity** to many underwriters
- Multiple small claims can get you non-renewed faster than one large claim
- Water damage claims are a particular red flag
- Even inquiries (not filed claims) may show up

## Auto Underwriting Factors

| Factor | Why It Matters |
|--------|---------------|
| **Age of driver** | Young drivers (16–25) and elderly (75+) have higher accident rates |
| **Driving record (MVR)** | DUI = major surcharge or decline. At-fault accidents, speeding tickets |
| **Years of experience** | New drivers = higher risk |
| **Vehicle type** | Sports cars, high-theft vehicles, luxury vehicles cost more to insure |
| **Vehicle age** | Newer = higher physical damage exposure; older = less to lose |
| **Annual mileage** | More miles = more exposure |
| **Territory** | Urban vs rural, state, zip code — affects theft, congestion, litigation trends |
| **Credit-based insurance score** | Strong predictor of claims frequency (controversial) |
| **Garaging location** | Where the car is kept overnight |
| **Use of vehicle** | Commute vs pleasure vs business |
| **Prior insurance** | Gaps in coverage are a red flag |

## Adverse Selection

**The problem**: People who know they're high-risk are MORE likely to seek insurance. People who know they're low-risk are MORE likely to skip it or buy less.

**Why it matters**: If an insurer attracts mostly high-risk applicants, their loss ratios will be terrible.

**How underwriting fights it**:
- Risk selection (declining or surcharging bad risks)
- Proper classification (pricing that matches risk)
- Eligibility rules
- Rating factors that differentiate risk

---

## Credit-Based Insurance Scores

This is one of the most controversial underwriting tools:

**What it is**: A score derived from credit history data, designed to predict insurance loss likelihood. It is NOT the same as a FICO credit score.

**What it considers**:
- Payment history
- Outstanding debt
- Length of credit history
- Pursuit of new credit
- Credit mix

**What it does NOT consider**:
- Income
- Employment
- Race, religion, gender, national origin

**The debate**:
- **Proponents**: Strong statistical predictor of claims; allows more accurate pricing
- **Critics**: Disproportionately affects low-income and minority communities; correlation ≠ causation
- **Regulation**: Some states restrict or ban its use (California, Hawaii, Massachusetts, Maryland, and Michigan — full bans or significant restrictions depending on line of business)

---

## Regulatory Constraints on Underwriting

Underwriters can't consider whatever they want. Key restrictions:

| Constraint | What It Prohibits |
|-----------|------------------|
| **Fair Housing Act** | Discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin, familial status, disability |
| **FCRA** | Rules around using credit information; adverse action notices required |
| **State insurance regulations** | Vary widely — some restrict breed-specific dog exclusions, credit score use, claims-history weighting |
| **Redlining prohibitions** | Can't refuse to write or charge more based on neighborhood racial composition |
| **Genetic information** | GINA prohibits use in health insurance; some states extend to other lines |

## Practice Questions

**1.** What's the difference between moral hazard and morale hazard?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Moral hazard is dishonest intent — deliberately causing or exaggerating a loss for profit (arson). Morale hazard is carelessness because you have insurance — not criminal, just negligent ("I don't need to lock my car, I have insurance").
</details>

**2.** A homeowner files three small water damage claims in four years. The insurer non-renews. Why?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Claim frequency is a stronger underwriting signal than severity. Multiple water claims suggest an ongoing problem (old plumbing, poor maintenance) and predict future losses. Many insurers will non-renew after 2-3 claims regardless of size.
</details>

**3.** Why would an insurer care about a homeowner's credit-based insurance score?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Studies show credit-based insurance scores are statistically correlated with claims frequency. People with lower scores tend to file more claims. It allows more accurate risk segmentation and pricing — though its use is controversial and restricted in some states.
</details>

**4.** A 19-year-old buys a Corvette and wants minimum coverage. What underwriting concerns exist?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Multiple red flags: young driver (highest accident rate age group), sports car (high theft, high repair cost, encourages speeding), minimum coverage (possible adverse selection — why so little coverage?). Expect significant surcharges or possible decline from preferred markets.
</details>

**5.** What is a CLUE report and why does it matter?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
CLUE (Comprehensive Loss Underwriting Exchange) is a database containing 7 years of claims history for both homeowners and auto. It follows the person AND the property. Underwriters use it to identify frequent claimants and properties with loss histories. Even if you switch insurers, your claims history comes with you.
</details>
