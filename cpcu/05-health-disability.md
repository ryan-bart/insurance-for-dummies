## Why This Matters

Health and disability exposures are the most likely personal risks you'll actually face. A serious illness or disability can be more financially devastating than death — you're still alive, still have expenses, but can't earn income. Understanding these coverages rounds out the personal risk picture.

---

## Health Insurance Basics

### Key Cost-Sharing Terms

| Term | What It Means |
|------|--------------|
| **Deductible** | Amount you pay first before insurance kicks in (e.g., $1,500/year) |
| **Copay** | Flat dollar amount per visit or service (e.g., $30 for a doctor visit) |
| **Coinsurance** | Percentage split after the deductible (e.g., 80/20 = insurer pays 80%, you pay 20%) |
| **Out-of-pocket maximum** | Most you'll pay in a year; after this, insurer pays 100% |
| **Premium** | What you pay monthly for the coverage itself |

### How They Work Together

> You have a $1,500 deductible, 80/20 coinsurance, and $6,000 out-of-pocket max.
> You have a $20,000 surgery.
>
> 1. You pay the first **$1,500** (deductible)
> 2. Remaining $18,500 is split 80/20: insurer pays $14,800, you pay **$3,700**
> 3. Your total: $1,500 + $3,700 = **$5,200** (under the $6,000 OOP max)
>
> If costs kept climbing, you'd cap at **$6,000** and the insurer covers everything beyond that.

## ACA Marketplace Basics

The Affordable Care Act (2010) reshaped individual health insurance:

### Key Rules
- **Guaranteed issue**: Insurers must accept all applicants regardless of health
- **No pre-existing condition exclusions**
- **Essential health benefits**: 10 categories that all plans must cover (hospitalization, prescription drugs, maternity, mental health, etc.)
- **Metal tiers**: Bronze (60/40), Silver (70/30), Gold (80/20), Platinum (90/10) — the percentages indicate the plan's share of costs on average
- **Premium subsidies**: Available based on income (tax credits)
- **Individual mandate**: Originally required coverage or a penalty; penalty reduced to $0 federally in 2019 (some states still have their own)

### Plan Types

| Type | Network Flexibility | Referral Needed? | Cost |
|------|-------------------|-----------------|------|
| **HMO** | Must use in-network providers | Yes, need PCP referral | Lowest |
| **PPO** | In-network and out-of-network (at higher cost) | No | Higher |
| **EPO** | Must use in-network (like HMO) | No | Moderate |
| **POS** | In-network preferred, out-of-network available | Yes, need PCP referral | Moderate |

## Disability Income Insurance

This is the "paycheck insurance" — it replaces income when you can't work due to illness or injury.

### Key Definitions of Disability

| Definition | What It Means | Implication |
|-----------|--------------|-------------|
| **Own occupation** | You can't perform the duties of YOUR specific job | More favorable — a surgeon who can't operate is "disabled" even if they could teach |
| **Any occupation** | You can't perform the duties of ANY job you're reasonably qualified for | More restrictive — harder to qualify |
| **Modified own-occ** | Own-occ for initial period (e.g., 2 years), then switches to any-occ | Most common in practice |

### Key Policy Features

| Feature | What It Is | Typical Range |
|---------|-----------|---------------|
| **Elimination period** | Waiting period before benefits start (like a time deductible) | 30, 60, 90, 180, or 365 days |
| **Benefit period** | How long benefits are paid | 2 years, 5 years, to age 65, or lifetime |
| **Benefit amount** | Monthly income replacement | Usually 60–70% of pre-disability income |
| **Residual/partial disability** | Pays proportional benefit if you can work part-time | Varies |

**Key concept**: Longer elimination period = lower premium. It's like choosing a higher deductible.

### Tax Treatment
- **If you pay the premiums**: Benefits are **tax-free**
- **If your employer pays the premiums**: Benefits are **taxable income**
- This is critical for planning — a 60% benefit that's tax-free may replace the same take-home pay as 100% of salary

## Long-Term Care (LTC) Insurance

Covers the cost of care when you can't perform daily activities independently. This is NOT health insurance — it covers custodial care that health insurance won't.

### What It Covers
- Nursing home care
- Assisted living facilities
- Home health care
- Adult day care

### Benefit Triggers
Most policies require inability to perform **2 of 6 Activities of Daily Living (ADLs)**:
1. Bathing
2. Dressing
3. Eating
4. Toileting
5. Transferring (moving in/out of bed or chair)
6. Continence

Or a **cognitive impairment** (Alzheimer's, dementia)

### Key Features
- **Daily/monthly benefit**: e.g., $200/day or $6,000/month
- **Benefit period**: 2, 3, 5 years, or lifetime
- **Elimination period**: Usually 30–90 days
- **Inflation protection**: Critical — care costs rise faster than general inflation

### Why It Matters
- Medicare does NOT cover long-term custodial care
- Medicaid does, but only after you've spent down nearly all your assets
- Average nursing home cost: $110,000–$130,000+ per year (semi-private room median was $111,325 in 2024; private room $127,750)
- LTC insurance protects your assets and gives you choices about your care

## Medicare Basics

Medicare is federal health insurance for people 65+ (and some younger disabled individuals).

### The Four Parts

| Part | What It Covers | Funding | Cost to Beneficiary |
|------|---------------|---------|-------------------|
| **Part A** | Hospital/inpatient, skilled nursing (limited), hospice, some home health | Payroll taxes | Usually premium-free if you paid Medicare taxes 10+ years |
| **Part B** | Doctor visits, outpatient, preventive, durable medical equipment | General revenue + premiums | Standard monthly premium ($202.90/month in 2026, income-adjusted) |
| **Part C** (Medicare Advantage) | Combines A + B through private insurers, often includes drug coverage | Private plan premiums | Varies by plan |
| **Part D** | Prescription drug coverage | Premiums + general revenue | Monthly premium varies by plan |

### What Medicare Does NOT Cover
- Long-term custodial care (the big one)
- Dental, vision, hearing (original Medicare — many Advantage plans add these)
- Care outside the U.S.
- Cosmetic surgery

### Medigap (Medicare Supplement)

Medigap policies fill the "gaps" in original Medicare (Parts A & B):
- Covers copays, coinsurance, and deductibles that Medicare doesn't pay
- Standardized plans labeled **A through N** (not all available in every state)
- **Plan F** was the most comprehensive (no longer available to new enrollees after 2020)
- **Plan G** is now the most popular comprehensive option
- Cannot be combined with Medicare Advantage
- **Guaranteed issue period**: 6 months after enrolling in Part B (age 65) — no health questions

## Practice Questions

**1.** You have a $2,000 deductible, 80/20 coinsurance, and $7,000 out-of-pocket max. You have $30,000 in medical bills. What do you pay?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
First $2,000 (deductible) + 20% of the remaining $28,000 ($5,600) = $7,600. But the OOP max is $7,000, so you pay $7,000 and the insurer covers the rest.
</details>

**2.** You pay your own disability insurance premiums. You become disabled and receive $5,000/month in benefits. Is this taxable?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
No. When you pay disability premiums with after-tax dollars, the benefits are received tax-free.
</details>

**3.** What's the advantage of an "own occupation" disability definition over "any occupation"?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Own-occ is more favorable. You're considered disabled if you can't do YOUR specific job — even if you could do other work. A surgeon who loses fine motor skills qualifies even if they could work as a consultant.
</details>

**4.** A 70-year-old needs full-time help bathing, dressing, and eating due to advancing dementia. Does Medicare cover this?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
No. Medicare does not cover long-term custodial care. It covers skilled nursing for limited periods after hospitalization, but ongoing assistance with ADLs is not covered. This is where LTC insurance or Medicaid (after spend-down) applies.
</details>

**5.** What's the difference between Medicare Part C and a Medigap policy?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Part C (Medicare Advantage) replaces original Medicare — you get A and B benefits through a private insurer, often with extras (dental, vision, drugs). Medigap supplements original Medicare — it fills the cost-sharing gaps (copays, coinsurance) but you keep original Medicare. You can't have both simultaneously.
</details>
