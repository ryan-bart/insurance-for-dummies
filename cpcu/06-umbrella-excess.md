# CPCU 555: Personal Umbrella & Excess Liability

## Why This Matters

A personal umbrella policy is arguably the best value in all of insurance — massive amounts of liability protection for relatively low premiums. Understanding how umbrellas work, and how they differ from excess policies, is tested heavily on both CPCU and CIC exams.

---

## Umbrella vs. Excess: What's the Difference?

These terms are often used interchangeably, but they're technically different:

| Feature | Umbrella | Excess |
|---------|----------|--------|
| **Broadening coverage** | Yes — covers some claims that underlying policies don't | No — follows the underlying policy exactly |
| **Drop-down coverage** | Yes — can "drop down" when underlying is exhausted or doesn't apply | No |
| **Scope** | Broader than underlying policies | Same as underlying policies |
| **Self-insured retention (SIR)** | Applies when umbrella covers a claim with no underlying coverage | Not typically applicable |

**In practice**: Most personal "umbrella" policies are true umbrellas — they provide both excess limits AND broader coverage.

---

## How an Umbrella Works

The umbrella sits on top of your other liability policies:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────┐
│     Personal Umbrella           │
│     ($1M, $2M, $5M+)           │
├─────────────────────────────────┤
│  HO Coverage E  │  PAP Part A  │
│  ($300K)        │  (300/300)   │
└─────────────────┴──────────────┘
```

### When a Claim Hits

1. The **underlying policy** (HO or auto) pays first, up to its limit
2. If the claim exceeds the underlying limit, the **umbrella** picks up the rest, up to its limit
3. If the claim involves something the underlying doesn't cover but the umbrella does, the umbrella pays after a **self-insured retention (SIR)** — commonly $250 to $10,000 on personal policies

### Drop-Down Coverage

This is what makes umbrellas special. The umbrella "drops down" to provide primary-like coverage (after the SIR) for claims that:

- The underlying policy excludes
- Fall outside the scope of underlying policies
- Occur in situations where no underlying policy exists

**Examples of drop-down:**
- Libel or slander claim (excluded from HO in some policies)
- Liability from renting a vacation property abroad
- False arrest or wrongful detention claims

---

## Underlying Insurance Requirements

Umbrella insurers require you to carry minimum underlying limits. Typical requirements:

| Underlying Policy | Minimum Required |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Homeowners (Coverage E)** | $300,000–$500,000 |
| **Auto liability** | 250/500/100 or 300/300/100 |
| **Watercraft liability** | $300,000–$500,000 |

If you don't maintain these minimums and a claim hits, the umbrella insurer treats you as if you had the required limits — you're responsible for the gap.

---

## What Umbrellas Typically Cover

Beyond just higher limits, personal umbrellas often add coverage for:

- **Worldwide liability** (underlying policies may be limited to U.S./Canada)
- **Broader definition of insured** (may include household members more broadly)
- **Personal injury** — libel, slander, defamation, invasion of privacy, false arrest
- **Liability arising from volunteer activities**
- **Landlord liability** for rental properties

---

## Common Exclusions

Even umbrellas have exclusions:

| Exclusion | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Intentional acts** | Can't insure deliberately harmful behavior |
| **Business/professional liability** | Need a commercial policy |
| **Workers compensation** | Statutory — separate system |
| **Contractual liability** (some) | Assumed by contract, not by negligence |
| **Pollution** | Environmental liability is a different risk |
| **Communicable disease** | Emerged as exclusion post-COVID |
| **Aircraft/some watercraft** | Need specialized policies |
| **Property in your care, custody, or control** | First-party exposure, not liability |
| **War/terrorism** | Uninsurable |

---

## Pricing and Limits

Personal umbrellas are remarkably affordable relative to the coverage they provide:

| Limit | Approximate Annual Premium |
|-------|--------------------------|
| $1 million | $200–$400 |
| $2 million | $300–$550 |
| $5 million | $450–$800 |

Premiums vary based on: number of vehicles, properties, drivers, driving records, and underlying limits.

### Who Needs an Umbrella?

**Everyone with assets to protect** — but especially:
- Homeowners (slip-and-fall exposure)
- Dog owners (bite liability)
- Pool owners
- Teen drivers in the household
- Landlords
- Anyone with significant savings or future earning potential
- People active on social media (defamation risk)

---

## Self-Insured Retention (SIR)

The SIR is the deductible that applies when the umbrella provides drop-down coverage (no underlying policy responds):

- Ranges from **$250 to $10,000** on most personal policies (commercial umbrellas may be $10,000–$25,000+)
- Only applies to claims where the umbrella is broader than underlying
- Does NOT apply when the claim is covered by underlying policies — in that case, the underlying policy is the "deductible"

## Practice Questions

**1.** You have a $1M umbrella, $300K HO liability, and 250/500 auto liability. A guest sues you for $800K after falling at your home. How does this pay out?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
HO Coverage E pays the first $300,000. The umbrella pays the remaining $500,000. Total: $800,000 covered. The umbrella had plenty of room — only $500K of the $1M limit was used.
</details>

**2.** Someone sues you for defamation (you posted something on social media). Your HO policy excludes defamation claims. You have a $1M umbrella with a $10,000 SIR. Who pays?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The umbrella drops down because it covers personal injury (defamation) that the underlying HO excludes. You pay the $10,000 SIR out of pocket, and the umbrella covers the rest up to $1M.
</details>

**3.** Your umbrella requires you to carry 300/300 auto liability. You only carry 100/300. You cause an accident with $250,000 in BI to one person. What happens?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Your auto pays $100,000 (your actual limit). The umbrella insurer treats you as if you had $300,000 — so they pay anything above $300K. You're personally responsible for the $200,000 gap between your actual auto limit ($100K) and the required limit ($300K). The umbrella pays $0 on this claim.
</details>

**4.** A $2M personal umbrella typically costs roughly what per year?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Approximately $300–$550 per year. Personal umbrellas are one of the best values in insurance — massive coverage for a relatively small premium.
</details>

**5.** What's the difference between "drop-down" coverage and regular excess coverage?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Drop-down means the umbrella provides coverage for claims that the underlying policy doesn't cover at all (after the SIR). Regular excess just extends the dollar limits of the underlying policy. Drop-down is what makes a true umbrella broader than a simple excess policy.
</details>
