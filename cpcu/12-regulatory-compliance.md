## Why This Matters

Insurance is one of the most heavily regulated industries in the U.S. — and unlike banking or securities, it's regulated at the **state** level, not federal. Understanding the regulatory landscape is essential for anyone in the business and is a staple of CPCU exams.

---

## The State-Based System

### Why States, Not the Federal Government?

The **McCarran-Ferguson Act (1945)** (McCarran-Ferguson Act, 1945) established that states have primary authority to regulate insurance. Congress can override this, but the default is state regulation.

**What this means in practice:**
- 50 different sets of rules (NAIC, 2024) (plus D.C. and territories)
- Each state has a **Department of Insurance** (DOI) headed by a Commissioner
- Insurers must be licensed in each state where they do business
- Rates, forms, and market conduct rules vary by state

### The NAIC

The **National Association of Insurance Commissioners** is the coordinating body:
- Develops model laws and regulations that states can adopt
- Creates the NAIC Financial Condition Examiners Handbook
- Maintains databases (complaint ratios, financial data)
- Provides accreditation standards for state regulators
- **Not a regulator itself** — just a coordinator and standard-setter

## Key Areas of Regulation

### Rate Regulation

Rates must be:
- **Adequate**: Sufficient to pay claims and remain solvent
- **Not excessive**: Not gouging consumers
- **Not unfairly discriminatory**: Distinctions must be actuarially justified

(See [Rating & Pricing](09-rating-pricing.md) for the regulatory approaches: prior approval, file-and-use, use-and-file, open competition)

### Form Regulation
- Policy forms must be filed with and approved by the state
- States may mandate certain provisions or endorsements
- Some states use "deemer" provisions — if the regulator doesn't respond within X days, the form is deemed approved

### Market Conduct Regulation
States examine insurer behavior in the marketplace:
- **Sales practices**: Are agents representing products accurately?
- **Underwriting**: Is the insurer discriminating improperly?
- **Claims handling**: Are claims being paid fairly and promptly?
- **Complaints**: Are complaint levels abnormal?

Market conduct exams can be targeted (specific issue) or comprehensive (full review).

### Solvency Regulation
Ensuring insurers can pay claims:
- **Minimum capital and surplus requirements**
- **Risk-based capital (RBC)**: Required capital tied to the riskiness of the insurer's business
- **Annual financial statements** (statutory accounting — SAP, not GAAP)
- **Triennial financial examinations** by the state
- **Investment limitations**: Rules about what insurers can invest in
- **Reinsurance regulation**: Requirements for reinsurance arrangements

## Licensing

### Insurer Licensing
- **Domestic insurer**: Licensed in the state where it's incorporated (domiciled)
- **Foreign insurer**: Licensed in a different state than where it's domiciled
- **Alien insurer**: Domiciled in another country
- All must obtain a **Certificate of Authority** to sell insurance in a state

### Producer Licensing
- Agents and brokers must be licensed in each state where they sell
- Requires passing a state exam
- Continuing education (CE) requirements — typically 24 hours every 2 years (NAIC, 2024)
- Different license lines: property, casualty, life, health, surplus lines

## Unfair Trade Practices

State insurance codes prohibit specific behaviors. Common unfair practices:

| Practice | What It Is |
|----------|-----------|
| **Misrepresentation** | Making false statements about a policy's terms or benefits |
| **Twisting** | Inducing someone to drop one policy and buy another through misrepresentation |
| **Churning** | Replacing a policy primarily to generate commission |
| **Rebating** | Giving something of value (beyond the policy) as an inducement to buy (illegal in most states) |
| **Unfair discrimination** | Treating similar risks differently without actuarial justification |
| **Unfair claims settlement** | Denying claims without investigation, lowballing, unreasonable delays |
| **Defamation** | Making false statements to harm a competitor |
| **Intimidation** | Threatening an insured to prevent a legitimate claim |

## Consumer Protection Laws

### Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (GLBA)
Federal law requiring financial institutions (including insurers) to:
- Provide **privacy notices** explaining what personal information is collected and how it's shared
- Allow consumers to **opt out** of having their information shared with non-affiliated third parties
- Implement **safeguards** to protect personal information

### Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA)
When using credit information in underwriting:
- Must have a **permissible purpose** (insurance underwriting qualifies)
- Must provide an **adverse action notice** if you deny, cancel, or increase premium based on credit
- Consumer has the right to dispute inaccurate information

### State Privacy Laws
Increasingly stringent — California (CCPA/CPRA), other states following. May require:
- Disclosure of data collected
- Right to delete personal information
- Right to opt out of data sales

## State Guaranty Funds

What happens when an insurer goes insolvent (can't pay claims)?

### How They Work
- Each state has a guaranty fund (or association)
- Funded by assessments on all licensed insurers in the state
- When an insurer is declared insolvent, the guaranty fund steps in to pay claims
- **Limits apply**: Most states cap at $300,000 per claim; some allow up to $500,000 (NAIC, 2024) (varies by state and line)
- Only covers claims from residents of the state
- Only applies to admitted (licensed) insurers

### Key Points
- Guaranty funds are a **safety net**, not full protection
- Large claims may not be fully covered
- Assessments are passed through to policyholders as premium increases
- The insolvent insurer's assets are liquidated first; the guaranty fund covers the gap

## Residual Markets

For risks that can't get coverage in the standard (voluntary) market:

### FAIR Plans (Fair Access to Insurance Requirements)
- Created after urban riots in the 1960s (NAIC, 2024)
- Provide basic property insurance for properties that can't get coverage elsewhere
- Available in 35 states plus D.C. (III, 2024) (as of 2025)
- Coverage is often more limited and more expensive than the voluntary market
- Designed as a last resort, not a competitive alternative

### Assigned Risk Plans (Auto)
- Required in most states
- Drivers who can't get standard auto insurance are assigned to insurers
- Each insurer takes a proportional share of the residual market
- Premiums are higher; coverage is usually just minimum required limits
- DUI drivers, multiple-accident drivers typically end up here

### Beach and Windstorm Plans
- Coastal property pools in hurricane-prone states
- Provide wind coverage that voluntary market won't offer
- TWIA (Texas), Citizens (Florida), SC Wind and Hail, etc.
- Often the insurer of last resort for coastal properties

### NFIP
The National Flood Insurance Program itself is essentially a residual market mechanism — private insurers historically wouldn't write flood, so the federal government stepped in.

## Practice Questions

**1.** What federal law established that insurance is primarily regulated by states?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The McCarran-Ferguson Act of 1945 (McCarran-Ferguson Act, 1945). It provides that state laws regulating insurance are not preempted by federal law unless a federal statute specifically relates to insurance.
</details>

**2.** An agent convinces a client to cancel a perfectly good homeowners policy to buy a new one from a different carrier, using misleading comparisons. What unfair trade practice is this?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Twisting — inducing a policyholder to lapse, cancel, or switch coverage through misrepresentation. If the primary motive was generating a new commission, it could also be churning.
</details>

**3.** An insurer declines a homeowners application and cites the applicant's credit-based insurance score as the reason. What must the insurer do under FCRA?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Provide an adverse action notice informing the applicant that credit information was used, identifying the credit reporting agency, and notifying them of their right to obtain a free credit report and dispute inaccurate information.
</details>

**4.** An insurer goes insolvent with $50 million in unpaid claims. What happens to policyholders?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The state guaranty fund steps in. It pays covered claims up to state limits (most states cap at $300K per claim; some up to $500K) (NAIC, 2024). The insolvent insurer's assets are liquidated first, and the guaranty fund covers the remaining gap. The guaranty fund recovers its payouts through assessments on all other licensed insurers in the state.
</details>

**5.** A driver with two DUIs can't find auto insurance in the voluntary market. What's their option?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The assigned risk plan (also called the residual market or automobile insurance plan). The state assigns them to an insurer. They'll pay much higher premiums and likely get only minimum required coverage, but they can get insured and meet financial responsibility requirements.
</details>
