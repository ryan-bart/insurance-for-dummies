## Why This Matters

Claims are the moment of truth in insurance — it's when the promise on paper becomes real. How claims are handled determines customer retention, legal exposure, and insurer profitability. This is where underwriting decisions meet reality.

---

## The Claims Process

### Step 1: Notice of Loss
The insured reports the claim. This can come through:
- Agent/broker
- Call center / 800 number
- Mobile app or website
- Third-party claimant contacting the insurer directly

**Key**: The policy requires "prompt notice." Unreasonable delay can jeopardize coverage, though insurers must show they were prejudiced by the delay.

### Step 2: Assignment and Investigation
The claim is assigned to an adjuster who:
- Contacts the insured
- Inspects damage (in person, virtual, or via photos)
- Takes recorded statements
- Obtains police reports, medical records, repair estimates
- Determines if the loss is covered under the policy

### Step 3: Coverage Determination
The adjuster answers three questions:
1. **Was there a covered cause of loss?** (Is the peril covered?)
2. **Does an exclusion apply?** (Is anything excluded?)
3. **Are all policy conditions met?** (Did the insured fulfill their duties?)

### Step 4: Damage Evaluation
Determine how much the loss is worth:
- **Property claims**: Repair estimates, contractor bids, replacement cost vs ACV
- **Liability/BI claims**: Medical bills, lost wages, pain and suffering, future care
- **Total loss**: If repair exceeds a threshold (usually 70-80% of value), the vehicle or property is "totaled"

### Step 5: Negotiation and Settlement
- **First-party claims** (your own policy): Adjuster and insured agree on the amount
- **Third-party claims** (liability): Insurer negotiates with the claimant or their attorney
- Settlement can be lump sum or structured (periodic payments)
- If they can't agree: **appraisal** (for property) or **arbitration/litigation** (for liability)

## First-Party vs. Third-Party Claims

| | First-Party | Third-Party |
|---|---|---|
| **Who's claiming** | The policyholder | Someone else (the injured party) |
| **Which coverage** | Property, medical payments, collision, comprehensive | Liability (Coverage E, PAP Part A) |
| **Relationship** | Insurer ↔ their own insured | Insurer ↔ claimant (and their attorney) |
| **Standard** | Contract obligation | Legal liability (negligence) must be established |
| **Examples** | Your house burns; your car is stolen | Guest falls at your home; you cause an accident |

## Good Faith and Bad Faith

### Good Faith
Insurers have a legal duty to handle claims in good faith. This means:
- Conducting a thorough investigation
- Communicating promptly and clearly
- Providing a reasonable explanation for any denial
- Paying valid claims promptly
- Not requiring unreasonable documentation

### Bad Faith
When an insurer unreasonably denies, delays, or underpays a valid claim:

**Examples of bad faith:**
- Denying a claim without investigation
- Misrepresenting policy provisions to the insured
- Unreasonable delay in payment
- Lowballing damage estimates to force a cheap settlement
- Failing to inform the insured of appeal rights
- Not defending the insured in a liability claim

**Consequences:**
- Compensatory damages (the claim amount that should have been paid)
- Consequential damages (additional harm caused by the bad faith)
- **Punitive damages** (can be multiples of the claim — this is the big risk)
- Attorney fees
- Regulatory sanctions

## Subrogation

After paying a first-party claim, the insurer "steps into the shoes" of the insured to recover from the responsible party.

**Example**: Your parked car is hit by another driver. Your insurer pays to fix your car under collision coverage. Your insurer then pursues the at-fault driver's insurer to get reimbursed.

**Key points:**
- The insured must cooperate and not do anything to harm the insurer's subrogation rights
- Waiver of subrogation = the insured agrees not to hold the responsible party liable (this waives the insurer's right too)
- If subrogation is successful, the insured gets their deductible back

## Common Claims Scenarios

### Homeowners

| Scenario | Coverage | Key Issues |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| Kitchen fire | Cov A (dwelling) + Cov D (ALE) | Replacement cost vs ACV, code upgrade costs |
| Burst pipe | Cov A + Cov C (contents) | Sudden vs gradual (gradual water damage often excluded) |
| Slip and fall | Cov F (med pay) first, then Cov E (liability) | Was there negligence? Med pay is no-fault |
| Theft | Cov C (personal property) | Sub-limits on categories, proof of ownership |
| Wind/hail damage to roof | Cov A | Percentage deductible in some states, roof age limitations |

### Auto

| Scenario | Coverage | Key Issues |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| Rear-end collision (your fault) | Part A (liability) for their injuries/damage, Part D (collision) for your car | Comparative negligence? |
| Hit-and-run | Part C (UM), Part D (collision) | Must report promptly; phantom vehicle rules vary |
| Car stolen | Part D (OTC/comprehensive) | ACV valuation; gap coverage if you owe more than value |
| Passenger injured | Part A (liability) or Part B (med pay) | Depends on fault and coverage elected |
| Hail damage | Part D (OTC/comprehensive) | Often no deductible or lower deductible |

## Fraud Indicators

Adjusters are trained to spot red flags:

| Red Flag | Why It's Suspicious |
|----------|-------------------|
| Claim filed shortly after policy inception | Possible pre-existing damage |
| Insured is overly familiar with the claims process | May have done this before |
| Damage inconsistent with reported cause of loss | Story doesn't match evidence |
| No police report for a theft | Basic documentation missing |
| Inflated inventory lists | Padding the claim |
| Recently increased coverage amounts | Anticipating a loss |
| Financial difficulties | Motive |
| Uncooperative with investigation | Trying to limit scrutiny |

**Important**: Red flags alone don't prove fraud. They trigger deeper investigation.

## The Appraisal Process

When the insured and insurer disagree on the **amount** of a property loss (not whether it's covered):

1. Either party demands appraisal
2. Each side selects an appraiser
3. The two appraisers select an **umpire**
4. If the appraisers agree, that's the amount. If they disagree, the umpire breaks the tie.
5. Agreement of any two (appraiser + appraiser, or appraiser + umpire) is binding

This is a faster, cheaper alternative to litigation for property claim disputes.

## Practice Questions

**1.** You cause a multi-car accident. Two people are injured and your car is totaled. Which PAP coverages respond?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Part A (Liability) for the other drivers' injuries and vehicle damage. Part D (Collision) for your own vehicle. Part B (Medical Payments) for your own injuries and your passengers' injuries.
</details>

**2.** An insurer takes 6 months to investigate a straightforward kitchen fire claim, then offers 60% of the documented repair cost with no explanation. Is this bad faith?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Likely yes. Unreasonable delay on a straightforward claim plus a lowball offer without a reasonable explanation are classic bad faith indicators. The insurer could face compensatory damages, consequential damages, and potentially punitive damages.
</details>

**3.** Your parked car is hit. Your insurer pays $8,000 to fix it under collision. You have a $500 deductible. What happens next?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Your insurer subromates against the at-fault driver's insurer. You paid $500 (deductible) and your insurer paid $7,500. If subrogation is successful, your insurer recovers the $7,500 and you get your $500 deductible back.
</details>

**4.** A homeowner and insurer agree the house fire is covered but disagree on whether the damage is $80,000 or $120,000. How is this resolved?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Through the appraisal process. Each side selects an appraiser, the two appraisers select an umpire, and any two of the three can agree on a binding amount. This resolves disputes about the amount of loss without going to court.
</details>

**5.** A claim is filed 2 days after a new homeowners policy is purchased, reporting a roof leak that caused extensive interior water damage. What should the adjuster do?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Investigate thoroughly. This is a red flag (claim shortly after inception) suggesting possible pre-existing damage. The adjuster should inspect the damage, check for evidence of long-term water intrusion (staining patterns, mold growth), review the application and any prior inspection, and potentially engage an engineer. Pre-existing damage would not be covered.
</details>
