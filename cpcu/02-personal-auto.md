## Why This Matters

Auto insurance is the most widely held form of personal insurance in the U.S. Most states require it. The Personal Auto Policy (PAP) is the standard ISO form and the foundation for everything you'll see in personal auto.

---

## Personal Auto Policy (PAP) Structure

The PAP has six parts:

| Part | Coverage | What It Does |
|------|----------|-------------|
| A | Liability | Pays others for BI/PD you cause |
| B | Medical Payments | Pays medical bills for you and passengers |
| C | Uninsured Motorists (UM) | Pays you when the other driver has no insurance |
| D | Damage to Your Auto | Pays for damage to your own vehicle |
| E | Duties After an Accident or Loss | Your obligations |
| F | General Provisions | Policy mechanics |

---

## Part A — Liability Coverage

This is the core of the PAP. It pays for bodily injury (BI) and property damage (PD) you cause to others with your vehicle.

### How Limits Work

Liability is typically expressed as **split limits**:

> **100/300/50** means (ISO, PP 00 01, 2018):
> - $100,000 per person for bodily injury
> - $300,000 per accident for bodily injury
> - $50,000 per accident for property damage

Some policies offer a **combined single limit (CSL)** instead — one pool of money for all damages.

### Who Is an Insured?

This is a critical exam topic:

1. **You** (the named insured) — for any auto
2. **Family members** — for any auto (a resident of your household related by blood, marriage, or adoption)
3. **Anyone using your covered auto** — with reasonable belief of permission
4. **Anyone else** — for your covered auto, but only for liability arising from the acts of persons in #1 or #2

### What's a "Covered Auto"?

- Vehicles listed on the declarations page
- Newly acquired vehicles (must notify insurer within 14 days (ISO, PP 00 01, 2018); if the policy has no existing physical damage coverage, the window is only 4 days (ISO, PP 00 01, 2018) to request it)
- Trailers
- Temporary substitute vehicles (when your car is broken down)

---

## Part B — Medical Payments

Pays reasonable medical expenses for the named insured and passengers, regardless of fault.

**Key points:**
- Typical limit: $1,000–$10,000 per person (ISO, PP 00 01, 2018)
- Covers treatment within **3 years** of the accident (ISO, PP 00 01, 2018)
- Covers the named insured as a pedestrian hit by a vehicle too
- Does NOT cover injuries sustained in a vehicle with fewer than 4 wheels (ISO, PP 00 01, 2018) (motorcycles) unless it's a regular coverage vehicle

---

## Part C — Uninsured/Underinsured Motorists (UM/UIM)

### Uninsured Motorists (UM)
Covers you when the at-fault driver has no insurance, or in hit-and-run situations.

### Underinsured Motorists (UIM)
Covers the gap when the at-fault driver has insurance, but not enough.

**Key points:**
- Required in many states
- Limits typically match your liability limits
- Only covers **bodily injury** in the standard PAP (some states add property damage)
- Hit-and-run qualifies as "uninsured" — but you must report it promptly

### Stacking vs Non-Stacking
- **Stacking**: If you have multiple vehicles on your policy, you can "stack" (add together) the UM/UIM limits. Two cars with $100K UM = $200K available (NAIC, 2024).
- **Non-stacking**: You get the limit on one vehicle only, regardless of how many you insure.
- This varies by state — it's a common exam question.

---

## Part D — Damage to Your Auto (Physical Damage)

This covers damage to **your own** vehicle. Two types:

### Collision
Damage from impact with another object or vehicle, or overturning.

### Other Than Collision (OTC / Comprehensive)
Everything else: theft, fire, hail, flood, vandalism, hitting an animal, glass breakage, falling objects.

**Key points:**
- Each has its own deductible (commonly $250–$1,000) (ISO, PP 00 01, 2018)
- Pays the **lesser of**: actual cash value (ACV) or cost to repair/replace
- **ACV = Replacement cost minus depreciation**
- Collision with an animal = OTC (not collision) — this matters because OTC claims often don't raise your rates

### Transportation Expenses
If your covered auto is stolen, the policy pays up to **$30/day, $900 max** (ISO, PP 00 01, 2018) for rental or transportation while it's missing (ISO increased these limits from $20/$600 effective 2018). Coverage begins 48 hours (ISO, PP 00 01, 2018) after the theft. (For collision losses, this isn't automatically included — need a rental reimbursement endorsement.)

---

## Part E — Duties After an Accident or Loss

The insured must:
1. Notify the insurer promptly (who, when, where, how, names of witnesses)
2. Cooperate with the investigation
3. Submit to examination under oath if requested
4. Authorize the insurer to obtain medical records
5. Submit a proof of loss if requested
6. **Not voluntarily assume any obligation** (don't admit fault at the scene)

---

## Part F — General Provisions

Important ones:

- **Policy territory**: U.S., territories, possessions, Canada, and between their ports
- **Other insurance**: If you have coverage under more than one auto policy, coverage is prorated
- **Subrogation**: After paying your claim, the insurer can pursue the at-fault party to recover what they paid
- **Policy period/territory**: Coverage applies during the policy period within the policy territory

## Exclusions You Need to Know

| Exclusion | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Intentional damage** | Moral hazard |
| **Vehicles with fewer than 4 wheels** (ISO, PP 00 01, 2018) | Motorcycles need separate policy |
| **Vehicles used as public conveyance** | Rideshare/taxi = commercial risk |
| **Business-owned vehicles** | Commercial auto policy needed |
| **Racing** | Extreme risk |
| **Vehicles used as a residence** | Homeowners territory |
| **Non-owned vehicles used in auto business** | Garage policy territory |

## State-Specific Considerations

### Financial Responsibility Laws
Most states require drivers to demonstrate financial responsibility, usually through:
- **Compulsory insurance laws**: Must have minimum liability limits to register a vehicle
- **Financial responsibility laws**: Must prove ability to pay after an accident

### No-Fault States
Some states are "no-fault" — your own insurer pays your injuries regardless of who caused the accident (up to a threshold). In these states:
- Part B is often replaced by **Personal Injury Protection (PIP)**
- PIP is broader than Med Pay: covers lost wages, essential services, funeral expenses
- You can only sue the at-fault driver if injuries exceed a threshold (dollar amount or verbal — like "serious injury")

**No-fault states include**: Florida (transitioning to tort system July 1, 2026), Michigan, New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Hawaii, Kansas, Kentucky, Massachusetts, Minnesota, North Dakota, Utah (III, 2025)

## Common Endorsements

| Endorsement | What It Does |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Rental Reimbursement** | Pays for a rental car after a covered collision loss |
| **Gap Coverage** | Pays the difference between ACV and loan balance if the car is totaled |
| **Towing and Labor** | Covers roadside assistance costs |
| **Custom Equipment** | Covers aftermarket parts/accessories |
| **Rideshare Endorsement** | Fills gaps when using your personal vehicle for Uber/Lyft |
| **Agreed Value** | Sets a pre-agreed payout for total loss (common for classics) |

## Practice Questions

**1.** You lend your car to a friend. She causes an accident injuring two people. Whose insurance pays first?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Yours. Insurance follows the car first, then the driver. Your PAP provides primary coverage for anyone using your covered auto with permission. Your friend's policy would be excess.
</details>

**2.** You have 100/300/50 limits (ISO, PP 00 01, 2018). You cause an accident injuring three people. The court awards $120K, $90K, and $80K respectively. How much does your insurer pay for BI?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Person 1: $100,000 (capped at per-person limit) (ISO, PP 00 01, 2018), Person 2: $90,000, Person 3: $80,000. Total BI = $270,000 — under the $300K per-accident limit (ISO, PP 00 01, 2018), so the insurer pays $270,000. You owe Person 1 the remaining $20,000 out of pocket.
</details>

**3.** You hit a deer on the highway. Is this collision or OTC?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
OTC (Other Than Collision / Comprehensive). Contact with an animal is specifically classified as other-than-collision under the PAP.
</details>

**4.** You're in a no-fault state with PIP coverage. You're rear-ended and suffer $8,000 in medical bills and $3,000 in lost wages. Where do you turn first?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Your own PIP coverage. In no-fault states, your own insurer pays your medical and wage loss up to the PIP limit, regardless of who was at fault.
</details>

**5.** Your teenager borrows a friend's car and causes an accident. Is your teenager covered under your PAP?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Yes. Family members (residents of your household related by blood, marriage, or adoption) are insureds under your PAP for any auto — even ones not listed on your policy. Your coverage would be excess to the friend's policy (which is primary since it follows the car).
</details>
