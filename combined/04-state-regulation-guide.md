# State-by-State Regulation Guide

[Back to README](../README.md)

---

## Why This Matters

Insurance regulation in the U.S. happens at the state level, not federal. That means 50 states plus D.C. each have their own rules on how rates are set, what coverage is required, and how the market operates. For the conceptual framework behind state regulation — McCarran-Ferguson, the NAIC, solvency regulation, guaranty funds — see [Regulatory & Compliance](../cpcu/12-regulatory-compliance.md). This guide is the practical companion: the actual rules, state by state.

If you're an agent, underwriter, or claims professional, this is the stuff that determines what you can sell, what you must sell, and how much you can charge.

## 1. Rate Regulation by State

States use one of four main systems to regulate insurance rates. Here's what each means in practice:

- **Prior Approval**: Insurers must file rates and get state approval *before* using them. Slowest to market, most regulatory control.
- **File-and-Use**: Insurers file rates and can begin using them immediately (or after a short waiting period). Regulators can disapprove later.
- **Use-and-File**: Insurers can implement rates first and file with the state within a set period afterward. Regulators retain disapproval authority.
- **Open Competition (No File)**: No filing required. Insurers set rates competitively. Must maintain records and provide data on request.

Some states also use **flex rating**, where changes within a band (e.g., +/- 7%) don't need prior approval, but larger changes do. (NAIC, 2025)

## 2. No-Fault Auto Insurance: State-by-State

In **no-fault** states, your own Personal Injury Protection (PIP) pays your medical expenses and lost wages after an accident, regardless of who caused it. You can only sue the at-fault driver if your injuries exceed a **threshold** — either a dollar amount (monetary) or a description of injury severity (verbal).

For the PAP coverage mechanics and how PIP interacts with other coverages, see [Personal Auto Policy](../cic/02-personal-auto.md).

### No-Fault States Comparison

| State | PIP Minimum | Threshold Type | Key Features |
|-------|-------------|---------------|--------------|
| Florida | $10,000 (Florida DHSMV, 2025) | Verbal | PIP repeal enacted — effective July 1, 2026, PIP is eliminated and replaced by mandatory 25/50 BI liability and $5,000 MedPay (Florida OIR, 2025) |
| Hawaii | $10,000 (Hawaii DCCA, 2025) | Monetary ($5,000) | "Add-on" style — PIP required but tort rights mostly preserved |
| Kansas | $4,500 (Kansas DOI, 2025) | Monetary ($2,000) | PIP covers medical, rehabilitation, lost income |
| Kentucky | $10,000 (Kentucky DOI, 2025) | Monetary ($1,000) | **Choice state** — can elect tort option |
| Massachusetts | $8,000 (Mass. DOI, 2025) | Monetary ($2,000) | Credit score ban; formerly state-set rates |
| Michigan | Varies (see below) (Michigan DIFS, 2025) | Verbal | 2019 reform introduced PIP level choices; formerly unlimited |
| Minnesota | $40,000 (Minnesota Commerce Dept., 2025) | Monetary ($4,000) | Among highest PIP minimums in the country |
| New Jersey | $15,000 (NJ DOBI, 2025) | Verbal | **Choice state** — Standard vs. Basic policy; verbal threshold default |
| New York | $50,000 (NY DFS, 2025) | Verbal | Called "Basic Economic Loss"; SUM coverage required |
| North Dakota | $30,000 (ND Insurance Dept., 2025) | Monetary ($2,500) | PIP covers medical, lost income, essential services |
| Oregon | $15,000 (Oregon DCBS, 2025) | None (full tort) | "Add-on" PIP — no lawsuit restriction |
| Pennsylvania | $5,000 (PA Insurance Dept., 2025) | Verbal | **Choice state** — full tort vs. limited tort |
| Utah | $3,000 (Utah Insurance Dept., 2025) | Monetary ($3,000) | Lowest PIP minimum among no-fault states |

**Choice states** (Kentucky, New Jersey, Pennsylvania) let drivers pick between no-fault and traditional tort. The choice affects both PIP coverage and the right to sue. (III, 2025)

### State Spotlights

**Florida**: Through early 2026, Florida remains the only state where bodily injury liability coverage is not required for vehicle registration — drivers need only $10,000 PIP and $10,000 property damage (Florida DHSMV, 2025). However, the legislature passed PIP repeal legislation (effective July 1, 2026) that eliminates PIP entirely, requires 25/50 BI liability minimums, mandates $5,000 MedPay, and shifts Florida from a no-fault to a tort-based system (Florida OIR, 2025). Previous repeal attempts were vetoed by the governor. The current verbal threshold requires "significant and permanent" injury to sue. (Florida DHSMV, 2025)

**Michigan**: Had **unlimited lifetime PIP** for decades — unique in the nation. The Michigan Catastrophic Claims Association (MCCA) collected assessments from all auto insurers to fund catastrophic claims above $600,000 (Michigan DIFS, 2025). The 2019 reform (PA 21) introduced multiple PIP coverage options:
- **Unlimited** (still available)
- **$500,000**
- **$250,000**
- **$50,000** (Medicaid enrollees only)
- **Opt-out** (only if covered by Medicare Parts A & B, or qualifying health insurance)

Mandated premium reductions: 45% for $50K level, 35% for $250K, 20% for $500K (Michigan DIFS, 2025). Since the reform, Michigan drivers have saved an average of $357 per vehicle annually, and total MCCA assessments have dropped by $120 per insured vehicle (Michigan DIFS, 2025). BI minimum limits are 50/100 (since 2020), with a default of 250/500 — drivers must sign a waiver to select the lower 50/100 option. (Michigan DIFS, 2025)

**New Jersey**: Offers two distinct policy structures. The **Basic Policy** has very limited coverage ($15,000 PIP, $5,000 PD, no BI) (NJ DOBI, 2025). The **Standard Policy** includes the verbal threshold (must show permanent injury to sue) but drivers can elect full tort rights for an additional premium. The verbal threshold is the default — if you don't choose, you get it. (NJ DOBI, 2025)

**New York**: PIP is called **Basic Economic Loss** and covers up to $50,000 for medical expenses, lost earnings (80% of income up to $2,000/month), and other reasonable expenses (NY DFS, 2025). New York also requires **Supplementary Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist (SUM)** coverage — a unique mandate. The verbal threshold requires "serious injury" as defined in Insurance Law Section 5102(d), including significant disfigurement, bone fracture, permanent limitation of a body organ or member, or a medically determined injury preventing normal daily activities for at least 90 of the 180 days following the accident. (NY DFS, 2025)

**Pennsylvania**: The **limited tort** option saves roughly 15-20% on premiums (PA Insurance Dept., 2025) but restricts the right to sue to cases involving "serious injury." Many drivers don't fully understand what they're giving up. The **full tort** option preserves full lawsuit rights. Agents face significant E&O exposure if they don't clearly explain the difference. (PA Insurance Dept., 2025)

## 3. States with Unique Regulatory Environments

### California

California's insurance market is shaped by **Proposition 103** (1988) (CDI, 2025), the most restrictive prior-approval regime in the country:

- Rates must be filed with and approved by the elected Insurance Commissioner before use
- Insurers must justify rate increases in public hearings, and consumer groups can formally intervene
- **Credit-based insurance scores are banned** for auto insurance rating (since 1988) (CDI, 2025)
- Only three rating factors are permitted for auto: driving record, annual mileage, and years of experience (CDI, 2025)
- Territory (ZIP code) is allowed but cannot be the primary factor

**California Earthquake Authority (CEA)**: A publicly managed, privately funded entity that provides residential earthquake coverage. Most private insurers don't write earthquake in California — they offer CEA policies instead. Coverage includes dwelling, personal property, and loss of use, but with high deductibles (typically 5-25% of dwelling coverage) (CEA, 2025).

**FAIR Plan and the Wildfire Crisis**: The California FAIR Plan provides basic property insurance for homes that can't get coverage in the voluntary market. As wildfires have intensified, the FAIR Plan has grown dramatically — it's become the default insurer in many high-risk areas. In 2025-2026, Commissioner Lara's **Sustainable Insurance Strategy** required insurers to write policies in high-risk wildfire areas in exchange for permission to use catastrophe modeling in rate-setting (previously banned under Prop 103). A 2026 ballot initiative to repeal Prop 103 entirely is being considered. (CDI, 2025)

### Florida

Florida's property insurance market has been the most volatile in the country:

- **Citizens Property Insurance** is the state-created insurer of last resort. It peaked at 1.4 million policies in 2023 (Citizens, 2023) and dropped to about 851,000 by March 2025 (Citizens, 2025), largely due to tort reform
- **Assignment of Benefits (AOB) reform** (SB 2-A, effective January 2023) (Florida OIR, 2023) banned post-loss assignment of insurance benefits for new policies — this was the single biggest driver of litigation and premium increases
- **Sinkhole coverage**: Standard homeowners policies cover only "catastrophic ground cover collapse" (structurally damaging sinkholes). Broader sinkhole coverage is available by endorsement but is expensive and heavily litigated
- **Hurricane deductibles**: Mandatory percentage-based deductibles for hurricane losses (typically 2-5% of dwelling value) (Florida OIR, 2025)
- **Auto BI liability transition**: Florida has been the only state not requiring bodily injury liability coverage. The PIP repeal (effective July 1, 2026) mandates 25/50 BI liability and $5,000 MedPay, replacing the current PIP-only system (Florida OIR, 2025)

Citizens' actuarially indicated rate for HO-3 policies dropped from $6,347 (2024) to $3,617 (2026) — a 43% reduction attributed to the litigation reforms (Citizens, 2025; Florida OIR, 2025).

### Texas

- **TWIA (Texas Windstorm Insurance Association)**: Created in 1971 (Texas DOI, 2025) after hurricanes constricted the coastal market. Provides wind and hail coverage for 14 coastal counties (Texas DOI, 2025) where voluntary market coverage is limited. TWIA rates are filed annually with TDI and subject to prior approval for increases (requires 2/3 board vote). Actuarially, TWIA rates would need to increase 38% (residential) and 45% (commercial) to be adequate (TWIA, 2026), but political pressure keeps them suppressed — the board voted for 0% increase in 2026 (TWIA, 2026).
- **Rate regulation**: Texas moved from prescribed (state-set) rates to **file-and-use** in 2003 with HB 2427 (Texas DOI, 2025). Before that, the state insurance commissioner literally set the rates insurers had to charge.
- Texas requires 30/60/25 minimum liability limits — higher than most states (Texas DOI, 2025)

### Michigan

Beyond the PIP reform covered above, Michigan's regulatory environment stands out for:
- **Credit score ban**: Michigan banned credit-based insurance scoring for auto in 2020 as part of the no-fault reform (Michigan DIFS, 2025)
- The MCCA (Michigan Catastrophic Claims Association) is unique nationally — it's a mandatory reinsurance mechanism that pools catastrophic auto injury claims above a retention level
- Michigan is a file-and-use state for both auto and homeowners (Michigan DIFS, 2025)

### Louisiana

Louisiana consistently has some of the **highest auto insurance premiums** in the nation (averaging over $4,100/year for full coverage) (Bankrate, 2026):

- **Direct action statute**: Louisiana is one of the few states where an injured party can sue the at-fault driver's insurer directly, without first suing the driver. This increases litigation costs
- **No-pay, no-play law**: Uninsured drivers face severe restrictions on recovering damages. HB 434 (2025) increased the penalty threshold from $15,000 to **$100,000** for bodily injury and from $25,000 to $100,000 for property damage (Louisiana DOI, 2025) — meaning uninsured drivers can't recover the first $100,000 of their BI or PD claim
- Louisiana shifted from prior approval to **file-and-use** in 2025 (SB 295) (Louisiana DOI, 2025) to speed up rate adjustments
- Minimum limits: 15/30/25 (Louisiana DOI, 2025)

### Massachusetts

- **Formerly the only fully managed auto market**: Until April 2008, the insurance commissioner set all auto rates (Mass. DOI, 2025). Massachusetts was the last state to adopt competitive rating
- The 2008 reform moved to **managed competition** (file-and-use), resulting in $270 million in consumer savings in year one and nine new insurers entering the market (Mass. DOI, 2025)
- **Credit score ban**: Massachusetts bans credit-based insurance scores for auto rating and underwriting — one of only four states with a complete ban (along with California, Hawaii, and Michigan) (Experian, 2025)
- Uses a **Safe Driver Insurance Plan (SDIP)** that applies surcharges and credits based on driving record (Mass. DOI, 2025)

### New York

New York has one of the most interventionist regulatory environments:

- **Strict prior approval** for both rates and forms, enforced by the Department of Financial Services (DFS)
- **SUM requirement**: New York is the only state that mandates Supplementary Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist coverage (up to $25,000/$50,000 must be offered, up to $250,000/$500,000 available) (NY DFS, 2025)
- Minimum liability limits: 25/50/10 with mandatory UM coverage at the same limits (NY DFS, 2025)
- DFS actively uses market conduct examinations and enforcement actions
- A 2026 bill (S5235) proposes banning credit scores and ZIP codes as auto rating factors (NY DFS, 2026)

## 4. Residual Markets in Detail

When the voluntary (standard) market won't cover a risk, **residual market** mechanisms step in. For the conceptual overview, see [Regulatory & Compliance](../cpcu/12-regulatory-compliance.md). Here's how they work in practice.

### FAIR Plans (Property)

**Fair Access to Insurance Requirements** plans exist in 34 states plus D.C. (NAIC, 2025). They provide basic property coverage for risks the voluntary market won't write — typically due to location, property condition, or high-risk exposure.

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Created** | Late 1960s, after urban riots made property insurance unavailable in many areas (NAIC, 2025) |
| **Funded by** | Assessments on all admitted insurers in the state, proportional to market share |
| **Coverage** | Usually more limited than standard policies — basic fire and allied perils |
| **Pricing** | Higher than voluntary market for comparable coverage |
| **Purpose** | Last resort, not a competitive alternative |

**Key state examples**:
- **California FAIR Plan**: Has become the de facto insurer in many wildfire-prone areas. Coverage limit was historically capped at $3 million for dwelling (CDI, 2025)
- **New York Property Insurance Underwriting Association (NYPIUA)**: Provides basic property coverage; often used in urban areas
- **Massachusetts FAIR Plan**: Covers basic property perils; supplements the voluntary market in older urban areas

### Beach and Windstorm Pools

Coastal property pools provide wind coverage in hurricane-prone states where the voluntary market has retreated:

| Plan | State | Key Details |
|------|-------|-------------|
| **TWIA** | Texas | 14 coastal counties (Texas DOI, 2025); separate wind/hail coverage; rates suppressed below actuarial adequacy |
| **Citizens** | Florida | Largest residual market insurer in the U.S.; writes wind and multi-peril; subject to depopulation efforts |
| **SC Wind & Hail** | South Carolina | Covers coastal properties for wind/hail only |
| **MWUA** | Mississippi | Mississippi Windstorm Underwriting Association; coastal wind coverage |
| **LCPIC** | Louisiana | Louisiana Citizens Property Insurance Corporation; wind and residential property |
| **Coastal Plan** | North Carolina | NC Insurance Underwriting Association provides wind coverage in 18 coastal counties (NC DOI, 2025) |

### Auto Residual Markets

Most states operate an **assigned risk plan** (also called an automobile insurance plan) for drivers who can't get coverage in the voluntary market:

- Each admitted auto insurer is assigned a proportional share of high-risk drivers
- Premiums are significantly higher than standard market
- Coverage is typically limited to state minimum requirements
- Common reasons for residual market placement: DUI, multiple at-fault accidents, young drivers with no experience

**Notable auto residual markets**:
- **North Carolina Rate Bureau**: Unique system where the Bureau files rates for the entire market and assigns risks through a reinsurance facility
- **Massachusetts**: Historically had the largest auto residual market (before 2008 reform); managed competition dramatically reduced the residual market population

## 6. State Coverage Mandates

### Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist (UM/UIM)

**States requiring UM coverage**: Connecticut, D.C., Illinois, Kansas, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Minnesota, Missouri, Nebraska, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New York, North Carolina, North Dakota, Oregon, South Carolina, South Dakota, Vermont, Virginia, Washington, West Virginia, Wisconsin (III, 2025)

**States requiring UIM coverage**: Connecticut, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Minnesota, Nebraska, New Jersey, North Carolina, North Dakota, Oregon, South Dakota, Vermont, Virginia, Wisconsin (III, 2025)

**States requiring both UM and UIM**: Connecticut, D.C., Illinois, Kansas, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Minnesota, Missouri, Nebraska, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New York, North Carolina, North Dakota, Oregon, South Carolina, South Dakota, Vermont, Virginia, West Virginia, Wisconsin (III, 2025)

In states where UM/UIM is optional, agents face E&O exposure if they don't clearly offer and document the client's acceptance or rejection of coverage.

### PIP / Medical Payments

**States requiring PIP**: Delaware, Florida, Hawaii, Kansas, Kentucky, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, New Jersey, New York, North Dakota, Oregon, Pennsylvania, Utah (III, 2025)

**States where Med Pay is commonly available but not mandatory**: Most tort states offer Med Pay as an optional coverage under the PAP

### Hurricane/Named Storm Deductible States

Nineteen states plus D.C. (III, 2025) allow or require percentage-based deductibles for hurricane or named storm losses:

Alabama, Connecticut, Delaware, Florida, Georgia, Hawaii, Louisiana, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Mississippi, New Jersey, New York, North Carolina, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, South Carolina, Texas, Virginia, D.C.

Percentage deductibles typically range from **1% to 5%** of the insured dwelling value (some go up to 10%). Triggers vary — some apply when a hurricane watch is declared, others when a storm is officially named. (III, 2025)

### Credit Score Restrictions

| Restriction Level | States |
|------------------|--------|
| **Complete ban (auto)** | California, Hawaii, Massachusetts, Michigan (Experian, 2025) |
| **Significant restrictions** | Maryland (can use for new policies, not renewals/cancellations) (Maryland MIA, 2025) |
| **Proposed bans** | New York (2026 bill S5235 pending) (NY DFS, 2026) |

All other states allow credit-based insurance scores as a rating factor for auto and/or homeowners, subject to FCRA adverse action notice requirements. (Experian, 2025; NAIC, 2025)

## 7. Practice Questions

**1.** A client in Michigan wants the cheapest possible auto insurance. What PIP coverage level should you discuss, and what's the catch?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

If the client is enrolled in Medicaid, they can choose the $50,000 PIP level (the cheapest option with mandated 45% premium reduction) (Michigan DIFS, 2025). If they have Medicare Parts A & B or qualifying health coverage, they may be able to opt out of PIP entirely. The catch: lower PIP limits mean the client bears more personal financial risk for medical costs after an accident. Agents must clearly explain the trade-off and document the client's choice — this is a significant E&O exposure area.
</details>

**2.** An agent in California wants to use a client's credit score to get them a better auto insurance rate. Can they?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

No. California bans the use of credit-based insurance scores for auto insurance rating entirely (Proposition 103, 1988) (CDI, 2025). Only three rating factors are permitted: driving record, annual mileage, and years of driving experience (CDI, 2025). California is one of four states (along with Hawaii, Massachusetts, and Michigan) with a complete ban on credit scoring for auto (Experian, 2025).
</details>

**3.** A Florida homeowner has a $400,000 dwelling policy with a 2% hurricane deductible. A hurricane causes $50,000 in damage. How much does the insurer pay?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

The hurricane deductible is 2% of $400,000 = $8,000 (Florida OIR, 2025). The insurer pays $50,000 - $8,000 = **$42,000**. Percentage-based hurricane deductibles apply to the insured value of the dwelling, not the loss amount (III, 2025). This often surprises homeowners who are used to flat-dollar deductibles — a $400K home with a 5% hurricane deductible has effectively a $20,000 deductible for storm damage.
</details>

**4.** An uninsured driver in Louisiana causes an accident and suffers $150,000 in injuries from the other driver's negligence. How much can they recover?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Under Louisiana's updated no-pay, no-play statute (HB 434, 2025), the uninsured driver cannot recover the first $100,000 of bodily injury damages (Louisiana DOI, 2025). They could potentially recover only $50,000 ($150,000 - $100,000 penalty). This is one of the harshest no-pay, no-play penalties in the country — previously the threshold was $15,000 (Louisiana DOI, 2025).
</details>

**5.** What makes New York's UM/UIM requirement different from every other state?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

New York requires **Supplementary Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist (SUM)** coverage — a mandate unique to New York. Insurers must offer SUM coverage up to $25,000/$50,000, with options available up to $250,000/$500,000 (NY DFS, 2025). SUM coverage goes beyond standard UM/UIM by providing additional protection layers. Other states require UM, UIM, or both, but none use the SUM structure that New York mandates.
</details>

**6.** Texas used to have state-prescribed auto insurance rates. What system does it use now, and when did it change?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Texas switched from prescribed (state-set) rates to a **file-and-use** system in 2003 with HB 2427 (Texas DOI, 2025). Before that, the state insurance commissioner literally dictated the rates insurers had to charge. The change allowed insurers to compete on price, and consumers could shop for the first time. However, TWIA (the coastal windstorm pool) still operates under prior approval for rate increases, requiring a 2/3 board vote before any increase can be filed (TWIA, 2026).
</details>
