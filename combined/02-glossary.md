# Glossary of Key Terms

[Back to README](../README.md)

---

**Actual Cash Value (ACV)**: The cost to replace damaged property minus depreciation. A 10-year-old roof costs $20K to replace but has depreciated by 50% — ACV is $10K.

**Additional Insured**: A person or entity added to someone else's policy for liability protection. Common in contracts (landlords added to tenants' policies).

**Additional Living Expenses (ALE)**: The extra costs you incur when your home is uninhabitable due to a covered loss — hotels, restaurants, etc. Covered under HO Coverage D.

**Adverse Selection**: The tendency of higher-risk individuals to seek more insurance, while lower-risk individuals buy less. This is the problem underwriting tries to solve.

**Agreed Value**: A valuation method where the insured and insurer pre-agree on the value of an item. At total loss, that amount is paid with no depreciation debate. Common for fine art, jewelry, and collectibles.

**Binder**: A temporary insurance contract providing coverage until the formal policy is issued. Usually effective immediately.

**CLUE Report**: Comprehensive Loss Underwriting Exchange. A database tracking 7 years of claims history for individuals and properties. Used by underwriters to evaluate risk.

**Coinsurance**: In health insurance: the percentage split between insurer and insured after the deductible (e.g., 80/20). In property insurance: a penalty clause if you don't insure to a required percentage of value (usually 80%).

**Combined Ratio**: Loss ratio + expense ratio. Below 100% = underwriting profit. Above 100% = underwriting loss. The key measure of insurance company operating performance.

**Combined Single Limit (CSL)**: A single liability limit that covers both bodily injury and property damage per accident, instead of separate split limits.

**Covered Auto**: Under the PAP, any vehicle listed on the declarations page, plus newly acquired vehicles, temporary substitutes, and trailers.

**Declarations (Dec Page)**: The front page of a policy showing the named insured, property/vehicle descriptions, coverage limits, deductibles, premium, and policy period. The "who, what, where, when, how much."

**Deductible**: The amount the insured pays out of pocket before insurance kicks in. Higher deductible = lower premium.

**Drop-Down Coverage**: A feature of umbrella policies where the umbrella provides primary-like coverage (after the SIR) for claims that underlying policies don't cover.

**Elimination Period**: The waiting period in a disability insurance policy before benefits begin. Like a time-based deductible. Typical: 30, 60, 90, or 180 days.

**Endorsement**: A document attached to a policy that modifies its terms — adding, removing, or changing coverage. Also called a rider (in life insurance).

**Excess Policy**: A policy that provides additional limits above the underlying policy, following the same terms and conditions. Unlike an umbrella, it does NOT broaden coverage.

**Exclusion**: A policy provision that eliminates coverage for specific causes of loss, hazards, property, or conditions. The policy says what it covers; exclusions take things back.

**FAIR Plan**: Fair Access to Insurance Requirements. State-run programs providing basic property insurance for risks that can't find coverage in the voluntary market. Created after 1960s urban riots.

**Floater**: An inland marine policy or endorsement that covers specific personal property on an open-peril, worldwide basis. "Floats" with the item wherever it goes.

**GLBA (Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act)**: Federal law requiring financial institutions (including insurers) to protect consumer financial information and provide privacy notices.

**Guaranty Fund**: A state mechanism that pays claims when an insurer becomes insolvent. Funded by assessments on other licensed insurers. Limits typically $300K–$500K per claim.

**Indemnity**: The principle of restoring the insured to their pre-loss financial condition — no better, no worse. Insurance pays for the loss, not a profit.

**Inland Marine**: Insurance covering mobile property, property in transit, and property of unusual value that doesn't fit standard property forms. Historical term from extending ocean marine concepts to land.

**Insurable Interest**: A financial stake in the property or person being insured. You must have an insurable interest to purchase insurance — prevents wagering.

**Loss of Use**: Coverage for the financial consequences of being unable to use your property after a covered loss. In homeowners: Coverage D (ALE). Not available under NFIP.

**McCarran-Ferguson Act (1945)**: Federal law establishing that states (not the federal government) are the primary regulators of insurance.

**Moral Hazard**: The tendency to be dishonest — intentionally causing or exaggerating a loss for financial gain. Example: arson for profit.

**Morale Hazard**: The tendency to be careless because you have insurance. Not criminal, just negligent. Example: not locking your car because "insurance will cover it."

**Named Peril**: A policy that only covers causes of loss specifically listed in the policy. If the peril isn't named, it's not covered. Opposite of open peril.

**NFIP (National Flood Insurance Program)**: Federal program providing flood insurance through private insurers. Max limits: $250K building, $100K contents for residential.

**No-Fault Insurance**: A system where your own insurer pays your injuries regardless of who caused the accident. Limits the right to sue. Used in about 12 states.

**Open Peril**: A policy that covers all causes of loss unless specifically excluded. Also called "all risk" or "special form." Broader than named peril.

**Other Than Collision (OTC)**: The PAP term for comprehensive coverage — theft, fire, hail, flood, vandalism, animal strikes, glass breakage, falling objects. Everything that isn't collision.

**PAP (Personal Auto Policy)**: The standard ISO personal automobile policy form. Six parts: Liability, Medical Payments, UM, Physical Damage, Duties, General Provisions.

**PIP (Personal Injury Protection)**: No-fault auto coverage that pays the insured's medical expenses, lost wages, and essential services regardless of fault. Broader than medical payments coverage.

**Premium**: The price paid for insurance coverage. Built from loss costs + expenses + profit loading.

**Pro Rata**: A proportional sharing method. If two policies cover the same loss, each pays its proportional share based on policy limits.

**Replacement Cost**: The cost to replace damaged property with new property of like kind and quality, without deducting for depreciation. More favorable than ACV.

**Retained Limit**: In umbrella insurance, the amount the insured must cover before the umbrella pays. When underlying insurance applies, the underlying limit IS the retained limit. When no underlying applies, the SIR is the retained limit.

**Rider**: A modification to an insurance policy. In life insurance, a rider adds extra benefits (waiver of premium, accidental death, guaranteed insurability).

**Schedule**: To individually list and describe items with their specific values on an insurance policy. Scheduled items get broader, itemized coverage.

**SIR (Self-Insured Retention)**: The amount the insured pays out of pocket when an umbrella policy drops down to cover a claim that the underlying policies don't cover. Typically $10,000.

**Split Limits**: Liability limits expressed as three numbers (e.g., 100/300/50): per-person BI, per-accident BI, per-accident PD.

**Stacking**: Adding together UM/UIM limits across multiple vehicles on the same policy. Allowed in some states, prohibited in others.

**Subrogation**: After paying a claim, the insurer's right to pursue recovery from the party that caused the loss. "Stepping into the shoes" of the insured.

**Umbrella Policy**: A liability policy that sits over underlying policies (HO, auto), providing both higher limits (excess coverage) AND broader coverage (drop-down) for claims the underlying policies don't cover.

**Underinsured Motorist (UIM)**: Coverage that pays the difference when the at-fault driver has insurance, but not enough to cover your injuries.

**Uninsured Motorist (UM)**: Coverage that pays for your injuries when the at-fault driver has no insurance at all, or in hit-and-run situations.

**Waiver of Subrogation**: An agreement where the insured gives up the right to recover from a responsible third party. This also eliminates the insurer's subrogation rights.

**Advisory Loss Cost**: The expected cost of claims per exposure unit, published by ISO/Verisk based on pooled industry data. Does not include expenses or profit — carriers add those via a loss cost multiplier. The foundation of most personal lines rates.

**Catastrophe Model (Cat Model)**: A computer simulation that estimates potential losses from catastrophic events (hurricanes, earthquakes, wildfires). The three dominant vendors are RMS (Moody's), AIR (Verisk), and CoreLogic (Cotality). Cat model output drives the catastrophe loading in premiums.

**CEA (California Earthquake Authority)**: A publicly managed, privately funded entity that provides residential earthquake coverage in California. Most private insurers don't write earthquake directly — they offer CEA policies instead. High deductibles (5-25% of dwelling coverage).

**Citizens Property Insurance**: Florida's state-created insurer of last resort for property insurance. The largest residual market insurer in the U.S. Writes both wind-only and multi-peril homeowners policies for risks that can't find coverage in the voluntary market.

**Credit-Based Insurance Score (CBIS)**: A score derived from credit report data, designed to predict insurance claim likelihood. Not the same as a FICO score. Impact can be 2-3x on premium. Banned in California, Hawaii, Massachusetts, and Michigan (auto). See [Rating Deep Dive](05-rating-deep-dive.md) for details.

**Loss Cost Multiplier (LCM)**: The factor a carrier applies to ISO advisory loss costs to add its own expense and profit provisions. An LCM of 1.35 means the carrier adds 35% on top of the pure loss cost.

**MCCA (Michigan Catastrophic Claims Association)**: A mandatory reinsurance mechanism unique to Michigan that pools catastrophic auto injury claims above a set retention level. Funded by per-vehicle assessments on all auto insurers in the state.

**Proposition 103**: A 1988 California ballot initiative that established strict prior-approval rate regulation, banned credit scores for auto rating, and limited auto rating factors to driving record, annual mileage, and years of experience.

**SERFF (System for Electronic Rate and Form Filing)**: The NAIC's electronic platform used in nearly every state for insurers to submit rate and form filings to regulators.

**Telematics**: Technology (OBD-II device, smartphone app, or connected car system) that collects real-time driving data — mileage, braking, acceleration, time of day — for use in auto insurance rating. All current U.S. programs are opt-in.

**TWIA (Texas Windstorm Insurance Association)**: Created in 1971 to provide wind and hail coverage for 14 Texas coastal counties where the voluntary market has retreated. Rates are filed annually with TDI.
